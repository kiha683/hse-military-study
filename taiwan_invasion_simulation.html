The Intelligence Warnings

In March 2021, US Indo-Pacific Command commander Admiral Davidson testified before the US Senate: "Within the next six years (by 2027), China is very likely to invade Taiwan." This assessment was based on 2027 being the critical year when Xi Jinping's third term approaches its end, creating political pressure to act.

In October 2022, US Chief of Naval Operations Admiral Gilday went further: "In my own assessment, it's not 2027 ‚Äî it could be 2022 or 2023." Then in February 2023, CIA Director Burns revealed: "We have intelligence that Xi Jinping has ordered preparations for invading Taiwan to be completed by 2027."

The August 2022 Crisis: When US House Speaker Pelosi visited Taiwan, China launched massive military exercises and fired missiles into six zones around Taiwan. Five missiles landed in Japan's Exclusive Economic Zone near Hateruma Island ‚Äî making "a Taiwan contingency is a Japan contingency" terrifyingly real.

Triggers for War

According to US government analysis, the triggers for China's invasion include: Taiwan declaring independence, Taiwan applying for UN membership, internal chaos in Taiwan, Taiwan acquiring nuclear weapons, or foreign military forces stationing in Taiwan. The author assesses that Taiwan's international activities being interpreted by Beijing as moves toward independence is the most likely trigger.

Xi Jinping's Ambitions

At the CCP's 100th anniversary in July 2021, Xi declared: "Resolving the Taiwan question and realizing China's complete reunification is the historic mission of the Communist Party." At the 20th Party Congress in October 2022, the political report stated: "We will never renounce the use of force, and we reserve the option of taking all necessary measures" ‚Äî targeting "external interference forces and the very few Taiwan independence separatists."

The 20th Party Congress installed Xi's loyalists in all seven Politburo Standing Committee seats and placed Taiwan operations specialist He Weidong ‚Äî former Eastern Theater Command commander ‚Äî as vice chairman of the Central Military Commission. The new Eastern Theater commander Lin Xiangyang was also elevated to Central Committee member. These personnel moves clearly prepare the command structure for a Taiwan operation.

The Taiwan Strait Barrier

The Taiwan Strait averages 180 km wide at its narrowest point of approximately 130 km. The Strait is one of the world's roughest waterways ‚Äî typhoons from June to October, the northeast monsoon from October to March, and the powerful Kuroshio Current create extremely challenging conditions for amphibious operations. Only April-May and October offer relatively calm windows ‚Äî about 60 days per year suitable for a cross-strait landing operation.

At any given time, an average of 240 ships transit the Taiwan Strait, making it one of the world's busiest maritime corridors. About 88% of the world's largest container ships pass through these waters, and 40% of global maritime trade crosses the South China Sea nearby.

Analysis
What China Learned from the Ukraine War

For the PLA, Russia's invasion of Ukraine (the "Special Military Operation") was an invaluable learning laboratory ‚Äî a treasury of experience in operations, organization, equipment, and military technology. The author analyzes six key lessons:

1. Electronic Warfare / –†–≠–ë

Russia's failure to suppress Ukraine's communications was a fatal blow. In 2014, Russia's EW systems successfully jammed Ukrainian forces during the Crimea annexation, but Ukraine spent eight years studying and countering Russian EW ‚Äî even using drones to physically destroy Russian EW equipment. Russia's unencrypted communications were intercepted; soldiers used personal cell phones that revealed operational plans.

China's takeaway: Strengthen jamming capabilities across wider frequency bands. Encrypt all military communications. Ban personal phones. Attack not just Taiwan's communication infrastructure but also foreign communication satellites Taiwan relies on. China's electronic communications industry is far more advanced than Russia's, making military application easier.

2. Air Superiority / –ü—Ä–µ–≤–æ—Å—Ö–æ–¥—Å—Ç–≤–æ –≤ –≤–æ–∑–¥—É—Ö–µ

Russia failed to achieve air superiority in the opening phase. Long-range strikes damaged some fixed radars, but Ukraine's mobile S-300 systems survived and remained effective. NATO AWACS flying over Poland provided real-time intelligence on Russian aircraft to Ukrainian defenders. Two months into the war, Britain's MoD assessed: "Ukraine still holds most of its air superiority."

China's takeaway: The PLA Air Force has overwhelming numerical superiority over Taiwan's air force and should secure air superiority over the Taiwan Strait in the initial phase. The challenge is breaking through Taiwan's air defense missile network over the island itself. Use suicide drones to hunt mobile SAM systems and MANPADS.

3. The Failure of Russia's Northern Offensive

Russia's fatal error was dispersing forces across three axes (north/Kyiv, east/Donbas, south/Kherson) instead of concentrating power ‚Äî violating the fundamental principle of concentration of combat power. With 125,000 combat troops split three ways (~40,000 per axis), Russia was actually outnumbered by Ukraine's 90,000 army plus 130,000 Territorial Defense forces (~73,000 per sector). Russia's assumption that Ukrainian resistance would collapse ‚Äî born from the bloodless 2014 Crimea success ‚Äî proved catastrophically wrong.

China's takeaway: Assemble overwhelming force ratios (4:1+ on ground). Concentrate forces rather than attack everywhere. Avoid urban warfare initially ‚Äî bypass cities, cut supply lines, wait for surrender ("When the persimmon is ripe, it falls on its own"). Use information warfare to demoralize Taiwan before invasion.

4. Logistics / –¢—ã–ª

Russia's logistics failure was catastrophic ‚Äî inability to repair damaged equipment, exhaustion of fuel and ammunition, troops going without food and water. Abandoned Russian equipment was captured and reused by Ukraine. Western semiconductor sanctions crippled weapons production.

China's takeaway: Establish massive logistics bases at Fujian ports from the start. Use civilian cargo ships for large-scale transport operations. Construct temporary docks on captured Taiwan shoreline. Once ports are captured, shift to rapid port unloading. Lay undersea fuel pipelines. Deploy logistics convoys under heavy naval escort ‚Äî this convoy operation is the key factor determining the success or failure of the entire invasion.

5. Information Warfare

Russia's disinformation and false flag operations were exposed early by Western intelligence sharing. China will conduct multi-layered cognitive warfare against its own population ("The majority of Taiwan's people support unification"), against Taiwan's people (amplifying social divisions), and internationally (framing the invasion as "an internal affair"). China's domestic surveillance technology far exceeds Russia's, enabling tighter information control.

6. Hybrid Warfare / –ì–∏–±—Ä–∏–¥–Ω–∞—è –≤–æ–π–Ω–∞

China will learn from Russia's 2014 Crimea playbook but apply it with far greater sophistication. The author describes a detailed scenario for the hybrid warfare seizure of Ishigaki Island: cutting undersea cables, massive cyberattacks, EW attacks from disguised civilian ships to jam police communications, fake emails luring police into bomb traps, then "Ryukyu Independence Group" paramilitaries (like Russia's "little green men") landing from a civilian aircraft, followed by armed forces from cargo ships.

Key Battle
The Battle of Yonaguni Island
"I know it's dangerous ‚Äî but this is real combat. We jump from 200 meters."
‚Äî Colonel Wakamatsu, 1st Airborne Brigade Commander (fictional)

Yonaguni is a gourd-shaped island just 28.95 km¬≤ with a population of 1,712. It lies 509 km from Naha, 127 km from Ishigaki, and only 110 km from Taiwan. On clear days, you can see Taiwan with the naked eye.

The recapture operation involves three JSDF units: the 8th Division's 12th Regiment Combat Team, the 2nd Amphibious Rapid Deployment Regiment, and the 1st Airborne Brigade ‚Äî commanded by the 8th Division commander.

The Night Jump (2200 Hours)

At Kagoshima Airport, 1,070 paratroopers wait fully equipped ‚Äî 18 kg main parachute, 7 kg reserve, 30 kg rucksack totaling 60 kg per man. Colonel Wakamatsu orders jump altitude reduced from the standard 340m to 200 meters ‚Äî dangerously low but ensuring rapid descent to avoid enemy fire. The jump master kicks the first trooper out the door ‚Äî paratroopers exit from both sides at one-second intervals. The operation takes 20 minutes for all aircraft.

Landing, cutting chutes, sprinting to assembly points. The 1st Airborne attacks toward Sonai settlement supported by heavy mortars. Mortar rounds crash with thunderous explosions; tracer rounds weave patterns across the night sky. China hadn't expected a counter-attack this soon ‚Äî and hadn't anticipated US-Japan-Australian joint operations.

At dawn, the amphibious force lands at Hikawa Beach. After three days of fierce fighting, JSDF recaptures Yonaguni ‚Äî a bloody victory with heavy casualties on both sides.

How the War Ends / –ò—Å—Ö–æ–¥ –≤–æ–π–Ω—ã

Was China's invasion successful? Technically yes ‚Äî the landing succeeded. China would never launch an invasion without confidence in success. But the execution encountered far more friction than planned.

The decisive factors: The success or failure of the invasion hinges on (1) ground operations after landing, and (2) maintaining sea supply lines across the Taiwan Strait. These become China's greatest trials.

The author references the CSIS wargame (24 iterations of a 2026 invasion scenario), which concluded: "China's invasion of Taiwan would fail." CSIS projected Chinese losses of 138 ships, 161 aircraft, and 22,000 casualties. Taiwan: 26 ships, half its aircraft, 3,500 casualties. US: 7-20 ships including 2 carriers, 270+ aircraft, 7,000 casualties.

However, Yamashita notes this report serves as political messaging: telling China "don't try it," telling Taiwan "fight like Ukraine," and telling Japan "sacrifice alongside America." The key precondition is immediate US intervention and Taiwan refusing to surrender early. If Taiwan surrenders before US forces arrive, China wins.

The Nuclear Question: If Chinese supply lines are severed and the PLA faces retreat or surrender, Xi Jinping ‚Äî whose political survival is tied to the war ‚Äî may resort to nuclear weapons. The worst case: US-China nuclear exchange. This is why both sides fight under rigid escalation controls throughout the simulation.

The author's predicted endgame: Taiwan is divided ‚Äî China occupies the western lowlands (containing most cities, industry, population), while Taiwan retains the eastern coast behind the Central Mountain Range, supported by US-UK-Australian naval power. The Taiwan Strait becomes a de facto "Chinese internal sea." A prolonged stalemate leads to secret ceasefire negotiations in Geneva.

Yamashita's final message (April 2023): "War produces neither victory nor defeat ‚Äî only devastated land and human sacrifice. To prevent Taiwan from becoming a battlefield, the utmost diplomatic effort is essential. As Clausewitz said: 'War is a continuation of politics ‚Äî diplomatic failure leads to war.' Defense capability is necessary as the backbone of diplomacy. We must thoroughly strengthen defense capabilities as a force to deter war."

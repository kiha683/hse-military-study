On June 28, 1914, Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary was assassinated in Sarajevo. Within weeks, the great powers of Europe plunged into the most devastating war the world had yet seen. Over four years of slaughter: 6,500 warships engaged, 1,000 submarines deployed, 2,000 aircraft used, land mines numbered 270 million. Some 15 million died.

But Qiao Liang asks a question that most historians overlook: Who financed this war? And who profited from it? (–ö—Ç–æ —Ñ–∏–Ω–∞–Ω—Å–∏—Ä–æ–≤–∞–ª —ç—Ç—É –≤–æ–π–Ω—É? –ò –∫—Ç–æ –æ—Ç –Ω–µ—ë –≤—ã–∏–≥—Ä–∞–ª?)

The capital interpretation of WWI: Before 1914, the European great powers were creditor nations ‚Äî Britain, France, and Germany were the world's financiers. After 1918, every single European power ‚Äî winners and losers alike ‚Äî was deeply in debt to one country: the United States. The war transferred the entire accumulated wealth of 400 years of European colonialism across the Atlantic in four short years.

World War I: Capital Flows ‚Äî From European Creditors to American Empire

Resources and Capital: The Overlooked Factors (–†–µ—Å—É—Ä—Å—ã –∏ –∫–∞–ø–∏—Ç–∞–ª)

Traditional history views WWI through the lens of nations, alliances, and battlefield tactics. Qiao Liang insists we must view it through capital. The real question is: which side had the resources and capital endurance to sustain a protracted industrial war?

Germany and Austria-Hungary, the Central Powers (–¶–µ–Ω—Ç—Ä–∞–ª—å–Ω—ã–µ –¥–µ—Ä–∂–∞–≤—ã), had formidable armies but were resource-poor. Britain's naval blockade slowly strangled their economies. Meanwhile, the Entente Powers ‚Äî Britain, France, Russia ‚Äî could draw on their colonial empires for raw materials. But even they discovered a terrifying truth: modern industrial warfare consumes resources at a rate that no single nation ‚Äî not even a colonial empire ‚Äî can sustain alone.

The Allied Powers: Blood vs. Capital (–°–æ—é–∑–Ω—ã–µ –¥–µ—Ä–∂–∞–≤—ã)

Germany poured the blood of its young men onto the Western Front but lacked the capital reserves to purchase war materials on global markets. As the British blockade tightened, Germany couldn't import rubber, oil, copper, or food. Its economy began collapsing long before its military did.

The Entente: From Creditors to Debtors

Britain entered WWI as the world's greatest creditor nation (—Å—Ç—Ä–∞–Ω–∞-–∫—Ä–µ–¥–∏—Ç–æ—Ä) and financial center. By 1918, it was deeply in debt to American banks. France was worse ‚Äî it had lent massively to Russia (which defaulted after the Bolshevik revolution) and borrowed massively from America. The entire European financial system collapsed inward.

Qiao Liang examines Paul Kennedy's famous 1987 thesis in The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers ‚Äî that empires decline through "imperial overstretch." America's attempt to maintain global military supremacy while its economic foundation shifts from productive industry to financial speculation is the classic pattern of imperial overstretch ‚Äî this time in financial rather than military terms.

Why Paul Kennedy Was Wrong ‚Äî And Why He's Now Right

Kennedy predicted American decline in 1987 based on military overstretch. He was premature ‚Äî the Soviet collapse gave America an unexpected windfall. But Qiao Liang argues that Kennedy's fundamental logic was correct, just applied to the wrong domain. America's decline will not come from military overstretch but from financial overstretch ‚Äî the virtual economy consuming the real economy.

The Internet: The Greatest Innovation Dissolves the Last Empire (–ò–Ω—Ç–µ—Ä–Ω–µ—Ç —Ä–∞—Å—Ç–≤–æ—Ä—è–µ—Ç –ø–æ—Å–ª–µ–¥–Ω—é—é –∏–º–ø–µ—Ä–∏—é)

Qiao Liang offers a counterintuitive prediction: America will be brought down not by an enemy, but by its own greatest innovation ‚Äî the Internet. His reasoning:

The Internet's essential characteristic is decentralization (–¥–µ—Ü–µ–Ω—Ç—Ä–∞–ª–∏–∑–∞—Ü–∏—è). As the Internet spreads globally, it inevitably promotes a multi-polar world ‚Äî undermining the unipolar order that sustains American hegemony. America cannot stop this process because it invented the very technology driving it.

Qiao Liang told American scholars: "You think China is America's strongest challenger. You're wrong. China is walking its own path, not challenging America. The real threat to America's future ‚Äî especially its global position ‚Äî is you, America itself."

Author's Preface
Taiwan's Critical Defense Choice

In 2008, Taiwan formally launched its transition to an all-volunteer military. Admiral Lee, then serving as Director of Strategic Planning at the Ministry of National Defense, was tasked with planning the "Jing Cui" program to downsize the armed forces — under the impossible mandate of "reduce forces, maintain combat power."

Unable to honestly justify this slogan through conventional means, Lee found inspiration in the U.S. military's emerging concept of innovation and asymmetric warfare. This planted the seed for what would become the Overall Defense Concept (ODC) — a comprehensive asymmetric defense strategy he would champion as Chief of General Staff from 2017 to 2019.

The book's true purpose is not about how to win a battle. It is about building credible asymmetric capabilities that convince Beijing's leadership that a cross-strait war should never happen and is not worth attempting.

Lee draws a crucial analogy: in the Russia-Ukraine war, the iconic weapons were not tanks but anti-tank missiles and attack drones; not fighter jets but air-defense missiles; not warships but anti-ship missiles. The sinking of the Russian cruiser Moskva by two Ukrainian Neptune missiles in April 2022 heralded a new era of warfare.

"Platforms cannot kill — munitions can." Facing China's Goliath, innovative asymmetric capabilities are Taiwan's David's sling.

Chapter 1
Our Distance from War
"The peril of people today lies in knowing safety but not danger, in being able to enjoy comfort but not endure hardship." —Su Shi, On Teaching People the Art of War

On February 24, 2022, Russia's armored forces invaded Ukraine. The question "Is Taiwan next?" began appearing in international headlines. For the first time in decades, the distance between Taiwan and war no longer seemed so far.

Optimistic Taiwan

Despite thousands of PLA missiles aimed at the island and near-daily military provocations, most Taiwanese remain inexplicably optimistic about avoiding war. Lee identifies four sources of this dangerous complacency:

1. "No Independence, No War"

Many believe that as long as Taiwan doesn't declare independence, China has no reason to attack. Lee dismantles this: for Beijing, unification is a "sacred mission" and a matter of principle with zero room for compromise. China's 2005 Anti-Secession Law reserves the right to use force — Taiwan independence is merely one trigger, not the only one. Beijing will not shelve unification indefinitely.

2. "America Will Surely Protect Us"

Polls showed 60%+ of Taiwanese believed the U.S. would intervene militarily. But Lee soberly points out: the Taiwan Relations Act contains no legal obligation to deploy troops. Taiwan is neither a treaty ally nor NATO member. The U.S.-Taiwan relationship, however warm, does not guarantee military intervention.

3. "The Cost of Invasion Is Too High"

While invasion would indeed be enormously costly, Lee warns against underestimating Beijing's resolve. In China, unification is a non-negotiable national goal backed by fervent nationalism. Only 16% of Taiwanese supported unification in 2022 polls, and the gap is widening — making peaceful resolution increasingly unlikely.

4. "Decades of Peace Have Bred Complacency"

Since the 1996 and 1999 crises, no military confrontation has penetrated public consciousness. PLA sorties around Taiwan became so routine that media coined the term "encirclement flights" — and the public developed threat fatigue.

The International View

At a 2019 CSIS debate in Washington, 66% of experts believed China would not invade Taiwan by 2035. After the debate, the number barely shifted to 62%. But Lee notes that after the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war, international assessment has shifted dramatically — the question is no longer if but when.

Strategic Map — Taiwan Strait & First Island Chain

Why China Hasn't Invaded — Yet

Lee identifies several historical reasons: the Taiwan Strait as a formidable natural barrier requiring the most complex form of military operations (amphibious assault); decades of internal instability under Mao; Deng Xiaoping's pragmatic focus on economic reform; and the relative optimism about peaceful unification during the Ma Ying-jeou era. However, since Xi Jinping took power in 2012 and proclaimed the "China Dream," unification has become an urgent goal. Xi's 2019 Letter to Taiwan Compatriots equated the "1992 Consensus" with "One Country, Two Systems" — signaling that Beijing understands peaceful unification is impossible and is preparing alternatives.

When Could War Happen?

The factors determining whether war erupts are: (1) the intensity of China's unification ambition, (2) the PLA's capability to invade, and (3) the U.S. factor. Lee notes that U.S. Pacific Command officials publicly warned that the PLA could achieve full invasion capability by 2027.

PLA Invasion Methods

Lee outlines a spectrum from gray-zone coercion to full invasion: military intimidation → blockade → seizure of offshore islands → joint firepower strikes → full-scale three-dimensional (air-sea-land) amphibious assault. The PLA is building capabilities across all these scenarios simultaneously.

Chapter 3
The Military at a Crossroads

This chapter is a candid self-critique of Taiwan's defense establishment — rare from a former Chief of General Staff.

Key Problems

Perpetually Insufficient Budget

Taiwan's defense spending has never kept pace with the threat. Despite recent increases, it remains around 2.5% of GDP — far less than what's needed for symmetric competition with China. Lee argues the issue isn't just money, but how the money is spent.

The Volunteer Military & Declining Birth Rates

The shift to an all-volunteer force coincided with severe demographic decline. Recruitment challenges mean the military can barely maintain authorized strength, let alone expand.

Inability to Let Go of Traditional Weapons

The military remains emotionally and institutionally attached to expensive platforms — fighter jets, destroyers, and tanks — that serve as status symbols but are highly vulnerable in a first strike and impossibly expensive to replace in sufficient numbers.

Arms Procurement Dilemmas

Lee provides an insider's view of U.S. arms sales: the U.S. only sells "defensive" weapons as it defines them; deals are shaped by U.S. domestic politics; key technology transfers are blocked; and Taiwan often ends up buying what America wants to sell rather than what Taiwan actually needs. Recent purchases of F-16s, M1A2 tanks, and Paladin howitzers all reflect traditional thinking despite rhetoric about asymmetric warfare.

Lee's core criticism: Taiwan talks about asymmetric warfare constantly but continues to spend the bulk of its defense budget on exactly the type of expensive, high-signature conventional weapons that the PLA is specifically designed to destroy in the opening hours of a conflict.

Chapter 4
Taiwan's America Complex

Lee examines Taiwan's over-reliance on the assumption of U.S. military intervention — perhaps the most dangerous single assumption in Taiwan's defense planning.

Why Taiwanese Believe America Will Help

Lee analyzes four dimensions: geopolitical (Taiwan's strategic position in the First Island Chain), legal (Taiwan Relations Act, Six Assurances), military (arms sales relationship), and psychological (recent anti-China policies creating a sense of security).

Hard Truths About U.S. Capability

RAND Corporation wargames from 2019 showed that in a conflict over Taiwan, the U.S. could lose — or at best achieve a pyrrhic victory. Lee cites RAND analyst David Ochmanek: "If China goes all-out to seize Taiwan, the U.S. would suffer badly." Even in victory, the U.S. would need to spend hundreds of billions maintaining permanent defense forces.

A former senior Obama-era Pentagon official told Lee personally: "If the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs tells the President that our carriers could be hit by anti-ship ballistic missiles within 1,500 km of China — do you think the President would still send them?"

Lee's conclusion on U.S. support: Taiwan must make itself defensible first — creating an asymmetric "porcupine" that is credibly capable of self-defense. Only then can Taiwan reasonably expect U.S. support. No nation will fight harder for Taiwan than Taiwan fights for itself.

Strategic Ambiguity vs. Strategic Clarity

Lee surveys the ongoing U.S. debate. Strategic ambiguity has kept peace for decades through "dual deterrence" — deterring both Chinese aggression and Taiwanese independence moves. But as the military balance shifts, some argue ambiguity increases risk by encouraging Chinese miscalculation. The debate remains unresolved, and Lee warns Taiwan cannot stake its survival on its outcome.

Part II
Rethinking Taiwan's Defense
A fundamental reassessment of what Taiwan's defense mindset should be, the dilemmas it faces, and the strategic logic of the Overall Defense Concept.
Chapter 5
The Defense Mindset Taiwan Needs

Lee presents a three-tier hierarchy of national security objectives:

1
AVOID WAR
The best strategy — ensure war has no reason to happen. Maintain stable cross-strait relations while building credible deterrence.
2
DETER WAR
Make the enemy believe the cost of war would be unbearable and victory impossible — through correct strategy, defensive capability, resolute will, and a resilient society.
3
WIN WAR
Only as a last resort — make the enemy's mission of seizing Taiwan fail. Not total military victory, but denial of the enemy's objectives.

The Roosevelt Doctrine for Taiwan

Lee criticizes two extremes in Taiwanese public discourse: the "pacifists" who oppose all defense spending (which encourages the enemy to act) and the "ultra-nationalists" who constantly provoke China (which goads the enemy to act). Instead, Taiwan should follow Theodore Roosevelt: "Speak softly and carry a big stick."

Four Pillars of Deterrence

Taiwan must convince Beijing it possesses: (1) the correct strategy, (2) credible defense capability, (3) resolute will, and (4) a resilient society. The ODC addresses all four.

Chapter 6
Taiwan's Defense Dilemmas & Solutions

Taiwan faces an interlocking set of defense dilemmas: gray-zone coercion, full-scale invasion threat, budget scarcity, time pressure, and social division.

The Priority Framework

Lee argues Taiwan must make painful choices about resource allocation:

1. Accept Gray-Zone Operations Calmly

Gray-zone coercion (daily PLA sorties, naval harassment) is not an existential threat. Taiwan should not exhaust its limited resources responding to every provocation with expensive fighter scrambles. Gray-zone activity actually serves as a buffer against direct military assault.

2. Prioritize Countering Full-Scale Invasion

The existential threat is amphibious invasion. Taiwan must redirect the majority of defense spending toward mobile, dispersed, precise, inexpensive, high-survivability weapons — coastal cruise missiles, short-range air defense, sea mines, micro missile boats, drones, and advanced ISR.

The "David vs. Goliath" principle: Taiwan doesn't need to match China's military — it only needs to make China's mission fail. The two sides have fundamentally different objectives: China must fully conquer and control; Taiwan only needs to prevent that conquest.

The New Hope: The Overall Defense Concept

Lee formally introduces the ODC as the solution to all five dilemmas — a strategy that works within Taiwan's constraints rather than ignoring them.

Part III
The Overall Defense Concept
The heart of the book: a detailed blueprint for Taiwan's asymmetric defense — force building, operational concepts, and territorial defense.
Chapter 7
The ODC's Innovation & Asymmetric Thinking
"When the balance of conventional forces makes defeat inevitable, asymmetric warfare is the weaker side's only chance."

What Is the Overall Defense Concept?

The ODC is a comprehensive defense strategy centered on innovation and asymmetric concepts. It develops low-cost, high-effectiveness, numerous, precise, high-survivability small weapons tailored to the Taiwan Strait battlefield to maximize Taiwan's defensive advantages and prevent the PLA from invading or politically controlling Taiwan.

❌ Traditional Approach

  • Seek air/sea superiority (impossible)
  • Prioritize expensive platforms (jets, ships, tanks)
  • Try to defeat enemy at every point
  • Bottom-up procurement (services compete for budget)
  • Cost-blind acquisitions
  • Centralized command only
  • Annihilation warfare mindset

✓ ODC Approach

  • Area denial (achievable threshold)
  • Prioritize small, mobile, lethal weapons
  • Focus on making enemy's mission fail
  • Top-down integrated requirement planning
  • Rigorous cost-benefit & opportunity cost analysis
  • Decentralized execution capability
  • Denial-based asymmetric warfare

Redefining "Victory"

The ODC's definition of victory: Making the enemy's Taiwan-seizure mission FAIL.

Taiwan doesn't need to destroy China's military. It needs two things: (1) prevent the enemy from establishing a foothold, and (2) even if they land, prevent effective control of Taiwan.

Seven Core Principles

1. Redefine Victory

From "destroy the enemy" to "make their mission fail."

2. Abandon Annihilation Warfare

Adopt asymmetric operations instead of symmetric force-on-force battles.

3. Replace "Control" with "Denial"

Taiwan cannot control the air or sea — but it can deny the enemy control. The threshold for denial is far lower than for control.

4. Attack the Enemy's Mission Capability

Target the enemy's strategic center of gravity — their ability to execute the invasion mission — rather than trying to destroy their entire military.

5. Shape Favorable Battlefield Conditions

Choose when and where to fight: where Taiwan has high survivability and complete kill chains.

6. Leverage Geography & Civilian Resources

The Taiwan Strait, Central Mountain Range, dense urban terrain, and abundant civilian infrastructure are all force multipliers. "Insufficient military resources + abundant civilian resources" is the ODC's philosophy.

7. Build a Credible Territorial Defense Force

Volunteer-based community defense units that demonstrate the population's will to resist — the ultimate deterrent.

The Micro Missile Boat Controversy

Lee devotes extensive space to defending the micro missile boat program — cancelled in 2020 after he retired. He rebuts every criticism: not a throwback to old patrol boats (these are AI-networked trimaran stealth craft); not redundant with coastal launchers (sea-based missiles create multi-axis threats); not too fragile for rough seas (if boats can't sail, enemy can't land either); not lacking radar (deliberate design — they receive targeting from the network, avoiding electromagnetic emission that reveals position). Lee calls the cancellation a politically-motivated decision that "wasted already-invested funds and eliminated a critically important asymmetric capability."

Chapter 9
Operational Concept: How to Execute the ODC

The ODC's operational concept has four sequential phases:

ODC Operational Phases — Cross-Strait Defense
1
Force Preservation (戰力防護)
Survive the opening barrage. Disperse, conceal, harden, deceive. Mobile assets scatter to pre-planned positions. Priority: keep asymmetric forces alive.
2
Littoral Decisive Battle (濱海決勝)
Strike the invasion fleet at its most vulnerable — mid-strait crossing. Deploy anti-ship missiles from coast and sea, unleash drone swarms, activate mine barriers.
3
Beach Annihilation (灘岸殲敵)
Destroy landing forces at the beaches with mines, loitering munitions, artillery, and prepared defenses. Taiwan's coastline favors the defender — few viable landing beaches.
4
Defense in Depth (縱深防禦)
If forces land, conduct endless mobile guerrilla warfare in urban terrain. Small, dispersed units with anti-armor weapons. No decisive battles — only continuous attrition. "The best method is to keep forces alive and maintain tactical flexibility."

Defense in Depth: The Critical Last Phase

Lee argues that if the PLA lands, Taiwan must avoid large set-piece battles that the PLA can win with air superiority. Instead, small independent units using wheeled vehicles, MANPADS, anti-armor missiles, and tactical drones should conduct endless mobile denial operations in Taiwan's dense urban terrain. "No city has strategic value worth defending to the last; what matters is keeping the fight going indefinitely."

This requires fundamental changes: replacing heavy tracked armor with wheeled vehicles, pushing weapons and authority down to company-level units, training for decentralized operations, and accepting that the army must transform from a conventional force into a modern guerrilla force.

Chapter 10
Deep Deterrence: Building a Territorial Defense Force

Taiwan's current reserve system is fundamentally broken — annual call-ups of reservists for cursory refresher training cannot produce meaningful combat capability. Lee proposes something entirely different: a Territorial Defense Force (TDF) modeled after successful examples worldwide.

The Territorial Defense Force Concept

Structure

Primarily volunteer civilians, organized by community, led by active-duty or recently retired special operations personnel. Small, mobile guerrilla units equipped with light weapons stored at local military, police, fire, and coast guard stations.

Mission

Not conventional city defense — but hit-and-run denial warfare. Blend into the civilian population, conduct ambushes, disrupt enemy logistics, make occupation impossible. "They don't even need uniforms."

The Real Purpose: Deterrence Through Will

"The most important aspect of the Territorial Defense Force is organization, not combat skills." Its existence communicates to Beijing: even if you break through our regular military, you will face an endless insurgency. The last mile of conquering Taiwan will be the hardest, and it will never end.

Ukraine as Proof of Concept

Lee points to Ukraine's Territorial Defense Forces — volunteer civilians who achieved stunning success against Russian regulars. "If Ukraine had expanded its territorial defense years earlier, imagine how much more effective they would be now." Taiwan must act now.

Lee acknowledges this concept will face "severe criticism" but responds: "Technical problems have always been an excuse for those reluctant to reform. The critical question is whether the government has the vision, determination, and courage to build an innovative, resilient national territorial defense mechanism."

"The only wrong choice is to do nothing." That would waste the lessons Ukraine bought with an unimaginably expensive war.

The Future of Taiwan's Defense
Implementation challenges, institutional resistance, and the urgent need for a strategic paradigm shift.
Chapter 11
Implementing the ODC: Practices & Challenges
"I know you face many obstacles, but the first person to try to change things always bleeds. It has always been so." —from the film Moneyball

Lee provides a remarkably candid account of implementing the ODC from 2017-2019 and the institutional resistance it faced.

Initial Reception

The ODC was warmly received by the U.S. government, think tanks, and scholars — and featured in multiple Congressional bills (Taiwan Assurance Act 2019, NDAA 2021). President Tsai Ing-wen publicly endorsed it in speeches at CSIS, Hudson Institute, and ASPI. Young military officers embraced the concept.

The Rollback

After Lee's retirement in July 2019 and the tragic death of his successor General Shen Yi-ming, the ODC was progressively abandoned. The 2021 Quadrennial Defense Review reverted to traditional "air superiority, sea control, counter-landing" strategy. The term "ODC" became taboo within the military. Meanwhile, the PLA continued to modernize relentlessly.

Eight Pillars of Implementation

Lee outlines what successful ODC implementation requires:

1. Leadership Must Transform Its Thinking

The biggest obstacle is decades of traditional mindset. The ODC is "fundamentally an economics approach to solving military problems within limited resources" — not just a tactical method.

2. Build an Asymmetric Defense Strategy

The current strategy — "Resolute Defense, Multi-Domain Deterrence" — is too vague to guide asymmetric force building. Taiwan needs an explicit asymmetric defense strategy.

3. Reform to Top-Down Force Planning

Replace bottom-up "balanced force building" (each service competing for budget) with integrated requirements-based planning driven by the Joint Staff.

4. Acquire Asymmetric Weapons

"Platforms cannot kill — munitions can." Prioritize survivable precision weapons over expensive platforms.

5. Restructure Force Organization

Align unit structures with asymmetric operations — smaller, more mobile, more self-sufficient units.

6. Develop Asymmetric Doctrine

Current doctrine copies U.S. military manuals designed for power projection — the opposite of what Taiwan needs. Lee personally oversaw drafting new joint doctrine before retirement; it was shelved after he left.

7. Create Flexible Operational Plans

"You cannot use old traditional plans to execute a fundamentally new operational concept."

8. Reform Training

Training must match actual wartime needs. Annual Han Kuang exercises need to return to rigorous command post exercises rather than becoming showcase fire demonstrations.

Why So Much Resistance?

Lee identifies five causes: (1) political priorities favoring visible, impressive weapons; (2) deeply ingrained traditional military thinking; (3) misunderstanding of what "asymmetric" means (the M1A2 tank was described as "asymmetric" in official documents); (4) service parochialism fighting over budget; and (5) bureaucratic obstruction at the highest levels.

Chapter 12
Toward a New Defense Strategy

In the final chapter, Lee argues that Taiwan stands at a historic crossroads in the U.S.-China great power competition. The window for building credible deterrence is closing.

Taiwan Needs a Strategic Paradigm Shift

Lee's final argument is built on three truths:

1. Taiwan cannot compete with China in conventional arms — and throwing more money at traditional weapons only triggers an arms race Taiwan will lose.

2. The future of warfare belongs to large numbers of small, intelligent weapons — not small numbers of expensive platforms. This trend favors Taiwan if it acts.

3. The ODC is not about winning a war — it is about making a war so costly and so unlikely to succeed that Beijing's leadership concludes it should never happen.

The Path Forward

Taiwan must simultaneously pursue three tracks: credible self-defense through asymmetric capabilities, international partnerships that make Taiwan's security integral to regional stability, and social resilience through a territorial defense force that demonstrates national resolve.

The ODC represents the only viable strategic framework for a small democracy facing an authoritarian great power with overwhelming conventional superiority. Its principles — survive, deny, attrit, persist — transform Taiwan's geographic disadvantages into advantages and its resource limitations into strategic clarity.

Admiral Lee's closing message: "This book's true goal is not how to fight a winning battle, but rather — by building credible and reliable asymmetric capabilities — to convince China's leadership that this cross-strait war should never happen, and is not worth attempting."

Why Taiwan Matters

Easton presents multiple reasons why China obsessively pursues Taiwan:

1. Legitimacy of the CCP Regime

The CCP views "recovering" Taiwan as essential to its historical narrative of national rejuvenation –Ω–∞—Ü–∏–æ–Ω–∞–ª—å–Ω–æ–µ –≤–æ–∑—Ä–æ–∂–¥–µ–Ω–∏–µ. The existence of a prosperous, democratic Chinese-speaking society directly across the strait is a living rebuke to the Party's claim that authoritarianism is necessary for Chinese civilization.

2. Strategic Geography

Taiwan sits at the center of the First Island Chain –ü–µ—Ä–≤–∞—è –æ—Å—Ç—Ä–æ–≤–Ω–∞—è —Ü–µ–ø—å ‚Äî the string of islands running from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines that constrains China's naval access to the open Pacific. Controlling Taiwan would break this chain and give the PLA Navy unrestricted access to the Pacific Ocean.

3. The Semiconductor Factor

Taiwan produces over 60% of the world's advanced semiconductors through TSMC. Control of Taiwan would give Beijing enormous leverage over the global technology supply chain.

4. Domino Effect

If Taiwan falls, Japan's southern flank would be exposed, the credibility of U.S. alliances across Asia would collapse, and countries from South Korea to the Philippines would be forced to accommodate Beijing's demands.

The Central Question

Easton frames the book around three sets of questions: (1) How do internal Chinese military writings depict the campaign to conquer Taiwan? (2) How does Taiwan's military plan to defend the island? How long could they hold out before American assistance is required? (3) What are the implications for American strategy in Asia?

Easton's core argument: The PRC is actively planning and preparing for invasion scenarios against Taiwan. The war plan is not theoretical ‚Äî it is maintained, updated, and rehearsed at the highest levels of the PLA. Deterrence of this invasion should be the primary objective of both Taiwan and the United States.

The Spy Who Saved Taiwan

Easton reveals a previously little-known episode: in the early 1950s, the Kuomintang's intelligence services uncovered and dismantled a CCP spy ring operating within Taiwan's military. Had this espionage network succeeded, it could have facilitated a communist takeover from within. The spy case, led by CCP agent Cai Xiaoqian, aimed to subvert Taiwan's military from the inside ‚Äî demonstrating that even at Taiwan's most vulnerable moment, the threat extended beyond military invasion to political subversion –ø–æ–ª–∏—Ç–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∞—è –ø–æ–¥—Ä—ã–≤–Ω–∞—è –¥–µ—è—Ç–µ–ª—å–Ω–æ—Å—Ç—å.

When Taiwan's President Lee Teng-hui visited Cornell University in 1995, Beijing was furious. The PLA launched missile tests into waters near Taiwan, simulating a blockade. President Clinton responded by deploying two carrier battle groups –¥–≤–µ –∞–≤–∏–∞–Ω–æ—Å–Ω—ã–µ —É–¥–∞—Ä–Ω—ã–µ –≥—Ä—É–ø–ø—ã near Taiwan ‚Äî the largest American military deployment in Asia since Vietnam. China deescalated, but the crisis fundamentally reshaped cross-strait dynamics:

After the 1996 crisis: China began sharply ramping up military spending. Advanced weapons systems poured in from Russia. The PLA was downsized and restructured to become more lethal. The probability appears to be growing that at some point in the 2020s, China's military could be ready to launch a cross-Strait invasion.

Mobilization Timeline

A PLA invasion would require weeks to months of visible preparation: moving hundreds of thousands of troops and equipment to coastal staging areas; loading thousands of ships; conducting large-scale amphibious exercises (which could serve as cover for the real operation); and restricting international shipping traffic near assembly areas.

According to a Taiwanese military textbook scenario presented to officer candidates: authoritative tactical warning information comes from American intelligence authorities, who confirm that Taipei's worst nightmare is about to come true ‚Äî the exercises are actually a cover story, and China's real intention is to invade Taiwan. Within hours of Washington's message, Taiwan's president orders the military to its highest state of alert. All reservists are activated, fully mobilizing millions of men.

Key insight: True strategic surprise against Taiwan is extremely difficult because the scale of preparations required is enormous. The PLA would need to mobilize so many forces, ships, and aircraft that concealment from satellite reconnaissance and signals intelligence would be nearly impossible. The real danger is not surprise, but rather the interpretation of warning signs ‚Äî analysts might see the indicators but dismiss or misinterpret them.

Problem 3: Who Leads the Attack ‚Äî Force Structure

The PLA would need to assemble the largest amphibious force in history ‚Äî far exceeding D-Day. The critical limiting factor is available shipping: landing ships, transport vessels, and civilian ships pressed into service.

Additional Planning Problems

Easton identifies numerous additional challenges the PLA faces: logistics and supply chain (maintaining forces across 130 km of contested water); command and control (the "fog of war" effect on centralized PLA command culture); homeland security (concern about ethnic unrest in Tibet and Xinjiang during wartime); regional complications (India, Japan, Vietnam could exploit a China distracted by Taiwan); and nuclear escalation risks (the possibility that a losing PLA might contemplate nuclear use).

PLA's nightmare scenario: Internal PLA writings express deep concern that a failed Taiwan invasion could trigger a "chain reaction" ‚Äî ethnic separatists in border areas, religious extremists, and foreign forces could all take advantage. The Force Preservation Challenge

Taiwan's military hides its most critical assets in hardened mountain bunkers, dispersed across civilian infrastructure, and concealed in caves and tunnels. The United States and China are firmly entrenched in what will likely be a long and intense strategic competition —Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–æ–µ —Å–æ–ø–µ—Ä–Ω–∏—á–µ—Å—Ç–≤–æ. What America Must Do

Easton argues the U.S. must:

1. Strengthen Taiwan's Military

High-impact arms sales conducted regularly and predictably. Technology transfers for asymmetric weapons. Integration of Taiwan into regional security architecture ‚Äî maritime domain awareness, missile defense, and joint training.

2. Forward Deploy Forces

Maintain credible military presence in the Western Pacific. Ensure rapid deployment capability to the Taiwan Strait. Strengthen alliances with Japan, South Korea, Australia, and the Philippines.

3. Reform Diplomatic Approach

Treat Taiwan with the respect and dignity it deserves as a democracy. Move beyond self-imposed restrictions that treat Taiwan as a pariah while treating an increasingly authoritarian PRC with deference.

4. Prepare for the Worst Case

The basic PLA assumptions for invasion include: (1) the U.S. will intervene, but can be slowed or deterred; (2) a rapid and decisive war is imperative; and (3) surprise and speed are critical but viewed as highly unlikely to fully achieve.

Easton's strategic principle: American strategists should prepare for the worst case, while using all instruments of national power to prevent conflict. The goal is not to fight a war over Taiwan ‚Äî it is to make war impossible by making the cost of aggression obviously catastrophic for the aggressor. –ê–º–µ—Ä–∏–∫–∞–Ω—Å–∫–∏–µ —Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏ –¥–æ–ª–∂–Ω—ã –≥–æ—Ç–æ–≤–∏—Ç—å—Å—è –∫ —Ö—É–¥—à–µ–º—É, –∏—Å–ø–æ–ª—å–∑—É—è –≤—Å–µ –∏–Ω—Å—Ç—Ä—É–º–µ–Ω—Ç—ã –¥–ª—è –ø—Ä–µ–¥–æ—Ç–≤—Ä–∞—â–µ–Ω–∏—è –∫–æ–Ω—Ñ–ª–∏–∫—Ç–∞.

Policy Recommendations

Easton's concluding recommendations for U.S. policymakers include:

For Taiwan:

Invest heavily in asymmetric defense capabilities ‚Äî mobile anti-ship missiles, sea mines, drones, hardened command-and-control infrastructure. Maintain a credible reserve force. Build social resilience and national will.

For the United States:

Treat Taiwan as the strategic asset it is. Conduct regular, predictable arms sales. Integrate Taiwan into regional security planning. Forward deploy sufficient forces to make intervention credible. Demonstrate resolve through exercises, ship visits, and senior-level contacts.

For the International Community:

Recognize that Taiwan's security is integral to regional stability. A Chinese conquest of Taiwan would fundamentally reshape the Indo-Pacific power balance in Beijing's favor, threatening freedom of navigation, democratic governance, and the rules-based international order.

Easton's final argument: There is every reason to expect that Taiwan, with American help, can meet the threat it faces. It is almost unimaginable that a force of several hundred thousand troops could be transported across the sea and maintained against Taiwan's superior ground forces. As long as Taiwan's air force and navy exist ‚Äî even if reduced to "fleets in being" ‚Äî they provide a powerful partner to the army in preventing amphibious landings. The prospect of war and peace hinges on what the United States and its allies do in the years ahead. –ï—Å—Ç—å –≤—Å–µ –æ—Å–Ω–æ–≤–∞–Ω–∏—è –ø–æ–ª–∞–≥–∞—Ç—å, —á—Ç–æ –¢–∞–π–≤–∞–Ω—å, –ø—Ä–∏ –ø–æ–¥–¥–µ—Ä–∂–∫–µ –ê–º–µ—Ä–∏–∫–∏, —Å–ø–æ—Å–æ–±–µ–Ω –ø—Ä–æ—Ç–∏–≤–æ—Å—Ç–æ—è—Ç—å —É–≥—Ä–æ–∑–µ.

Five Scenarios for the Taiwan Strait

Based on his research, Easton outlines five possible scenarios for how the cross-strait situation might develop:

1. Gray Zone
2. Blockade
3. Limited Strike
4. Full Invasion
5. Stalemate

Scenario 1: Prolonged Gray-Zone Coercion –ó–∞—Ç—è–∂–Ω–æ–µ –¥–∞–≤–ª–µ–Ω–∏–µ –≤ —Å–µ—Ä–æ–π –∑–æ–Ω–µ

China increases military, economic, and diplomatic pressure on Taiwan short of war. Daily PLA sorties around Taiwan, cyberattacks, economic coercion, international isolation. Goal: force Taiwan to negotiate on Beijing's terms without firing a shot.

Probability: HIGH. This is already happening and likely to intensify. It exhausts Taiwan's limited military resources and tests public resolve without crossing the threshold of armed conflict.

Scenario 2: Naval Blockade –ú–æ—Ä—Å–∫–∞—è –±–ª–æ–∫–∞–¥–∞

China declares a maritime exclusion zone around Taiwan, interdicting commercial shipping. Uses submarines, surface combatants, and mines to enforce the blockade. Taiwan, heavily dependent on imported energy and food, faces economic strangulation.

Probability: MODERATE. Less risky than invasion but still an act of war. Would likely trigger U.S. and Japanese responses. Taiwan maintains 90-day strategic petroleum reserves but would face severe shortages of food and raw materials.

Scenario 3: Limited Missile Strikes –û–≥—Ä–∞–Ω–∏—á–µ–Ω–Ω—ã–µ —Ä–∞–∫–µ—Ç–Ω—ã–µ —É–¥–∞—Ä—ã

Scenario 4: Full-Scale Invasion –ü–æ–ª–Ω–æ–º–∞—Å—à—Ç–∞–±–Ω–æ–µ –≤—Ç–æ—Ä–∂–µ–Ω–∏–µ

Probability: MODERATE-HIGH. This may be the most likely long-term outcome if deterrence holds.

The Foundation: "Goods for People"

The most fundamental arrangement of the US-Japan alliance –Ø–ø–æ–Ω–æ-–∞–º–µ—Ä–∏–∫–∞–Ω—Å–∫–∏–π —Å–æ—é–∑ is simple: if something happens, America defends Japan. In return, Japan provides military bases. This is what treaty negotiator Nishimura Kumao called "goods for people" (Áâ©„Å®‰∫∫„Å®„ÅÆÂçîÂäõ) ‚Äî Japan provides the "goods" (bases), America provides the "people" (military forces).

The original 1951 Security Treaty –î–æ–≥–æ–≤–æ—Ä –±–µ–∑–æ–ø–∞—Å–Ω–æ—Å—Ç–∏ 1951 –≥. was signed the same day as the San Francisco Peace Treaty. But it had a fatal flaw for Japanese national pride: it only stipulated Japan's obligation to provide bases ‚Äî it said nothing about America's obligation to defend Japan. America could use its forces for Japan's security, but was not required to.

The 1960 Treaty Revision ‚Äî The "Second Unequal Treaty Revision"

The history of the US-Japan alliance, Chijiwa argues, is essentially a "history of unequal treaty revision" –ò—Å—Ç–æ—Ä–∏—è –ø–µ—Ä–µ—Å–º–æ—Ç—Ä–∞ –Ω–µ—Ä–∞–≤–Ω–æ–ø—Ä–∞–≤–Ω–æ–≥–æ –¥–æ–≥–æ–≤–æ—Ä–∞ ‚Äî echoing Japan's Meiji-era struggle to revise Western-imposed unequal treaties.

The 1960 revision under PM Kishi Nobusuke was the critical milestone: it explicitly added America's defense obligation toward Japan (Article 5) and introduced the Prior Consultation system –°–∏—Å—Ç–µ–º–∞ –ø—Ä–µ–¥–≤–∞—Ä–∏—Ç–µ–ª—å–Ω—ã—Ö –∫–æ–Ω—Å—É–ª—å—Ç–∞—Ü–∏–π to give Japan a voice in how US forces used Japanese bases.

The "Far East Clause" (Ê•µÊù±Êù°È†Ö) –°—Ç–∞—Ç—å—è –æ –î–∞–ª—å–Ω–µ–º –í–æ—Å—Ç–æ–∫–µ: The treaty allows US forces to use Japanese bases not only for Japan's defense, but also for "contributing to peace and security in the Far East." This clause reveals the alliance's true nature ‚Äî it is not just a bilateral arrangement, but is embedded in the regional order.

The Korean Secret Agreement

During the 1960 revision, a secret protocol called the "Korean Minutes" (ÊúùÈÆÆË≠∞‰∫ãÈå≤) ¬´–ö–æ—Ä–µ–π—Å–∫–∏–π –ø—Ä–æ—Ç–æ–∫–æ–ª¬ª (—Å–µ–∫—Ä–µ—Ç–Ω–æ–µ —Å–æ–≥–ª–∞—à–µ–Ω–∏–µ) was agreed between Japan and the US. It exempted US forces from prior consultation when conducting direct combat operations from Japanese bases in the event of a Korean contingency ‚Äî because they would be acting as "UN forces."

This secret deal was necessary because: (1) Japan needed to appear to have control over US base usage (for domestic politics), but (2) the US needed military flexibility in Korea (for strategic reality). The result was a classic Japanese diplomatic compromise: publicly, the Prior Consultation system maintained the appearance of Japanese sovereignty; secretly, the Korean Minutes ensured American military effectiveness.

Strategic Map ‚Äî The "Far East 1905 System" & Hub-and-Spokes Alliance

The "Far East 1905 System"

Chijiwa introduces a bold conceptual framework: the "Far East 1905 System" (Ê•µÊù±‰∏Ä‰πù„Äá‰∫îÂπ¥‰ΩìÂà∂) ¬´–î–∞–ª—å–Ω–µ–≤–æ—Å—Ç–æ—á–Ω–∞—è —Å–∏—Å—Ç–µ–º–∞ 1905 –≥–æ–¥–∞¬ª. Since the early 20th century, the regional order in the Far East has been premised on: (1) China being weak or self-restrained, and (2) Japan, Korea (at least its southern part), and Taiwan being "gripped" in the same camp by a dominant power.

Before 1945, this power was the Japanese Empire itself ‚Äî which held Korea as a colony (since 1905/1910) and Taiwan (since 1895). After Japan's defeat in WWII, a "power vacuum" emerged. The Korean War (1950-53) was the violent process by which the maintenance of this system was agreed upon. After the war, America replaced Japan as the guarantor of this regional order through what Chijiwa calls the "US-Japan / US-ROK Dual Alliance" (Á±≥Êó•„ÉªÁ±≥Èüì‰∏°ÂêåÁõü) ¬´–î–≤–æ–π–Ω–æ–π –∞–ª—å—è–Ω—Å: –°–®–ê-–Ø–ø–æ–Ω–∏—è / –°–®–ê-–Æ–∂–Ω–∞—è –ö–æ—Ä–µ—è¬ª.

The US-Japan alliance is NOT merely a bilateral "base alliance." It is a component of a broader security system ‚Äî the "US-Japan / US-ROK Dual Alliance" ‚Äî that sustains the regional order in Northeast Asia. The Far East Clause and Korean Secret Agreement are the "hinges" („Å°„Çá„ÅÜ„Å§„Åå„ÅÑ) connecting these two alliances. The alliance is inherently "open" to the Far East region, not "closed" as a bilateral deal.

Japan's "One-Country Pacifism" vs. Strategic Reality

Chijiwa identifies a fundamental tension: postwar Japanese public opinion has been shaped by "one-country pacifism" (‰∏ÄÂõΩÂπ≥Âíå‰∏ªÁæ©) –ü–∞—Ü–∏—Ñ–∏–∑–º ¬´–æ–¥–Ω–æ–π —Å—Ç—Ä–∞–Ω—ã¬ª ‚Äî a mindset that views security through a strictly domestic lens. This means: Japan accepts America defending Japan, but recoils from any connection to America's broader Far East defense commitments. Japanese feared being "dragged into a foreign war" through the Far East Clause.

But this attitude is incompatible with the strategic reality that the US-Japan alliance is a function of the "US-Japan / US-ROK Dual Alliance" sustaining the "Far East 1905 System." The Korean Minutes were born from this contradiction: Japan needed to appear to restrict US forces (to satisfy domestic opinion) while secretly allowing them freedom of action (to maintain alliance effectiveness).

The core insight of Chapter 1: The boundary-drawing problem isn't just about Article 9. Even if Article 9 were amended, Japan's instinct to draw lines between "Japan" and "everything beyond Japan" would not automatically disappear. This deeper attitude, rooted in one-country pacifism, is the real constraint.

What Article 9 Actually Says

Article 9 –°—Ç–∞—Ç—å—è 9 –ö–æ–Ω—Å—Ç–∏—Ç—É—Ü–∏–∏ –Ø–ø–æ–Ω–∏–∏ is commonly called the "war renunciation" clause, but technically it is a "war potential non-possession" clause. Paragraph 1 renounces aggressive war ‚Äî which is actually unremarkable under modern international law. The "Sacrificial Stone" ‚Äî How Collective Self-Defense Became Unconstitutional

Here Chijiwa reveals a stunning piece of institutional history. The ban on exercising collective self-defense –ö–æ–ª–ª–µ–∫—Ç–∏–≤–Ω–∞—è —Å–∞–º–æ–æ–±–æ—Ä–æ–Ω–∞ ‚Äî which dominated Japanese security debate for decades ‚Äî was not derived from a principled constitutional interpretation. It was a political expedient ‚Äî a "sleight of hand" (ÊâãÂìÅ).

In the early 1950s, the newly-created SDF's constitutionality was extremely fragile. To defend it, the government needed to draw a clear line defining "minimum necessary force." They chose collective self-defense as the "sacrificial stone" (Êç®„Å¶Áü≥) ¬´–∫–∞–º–µ–Ω—å, –ø—Ä–∏–Ω–µ—Å—ë–Ω–Ω—ã–π –≤ –∂–µ—Ä—Ç–≤—É¬ª: by declaring that exercising collective self-defense was beyond the minimum necessary, they made the boundary of constitutionality clearer, thereby protecting the SDF's very existence.

The paradox: Collective self-defense was declared unconstitutional not because of a careful constitutional analysis, but as a political bargain to save the SDF from being declared unconstitutional itself. A stopgap measure from the 1950s became an unshakeable constitutional principle for over 60 years.

The Hidden Bargain Behind Article 9

Even the adoption of Article 9 itself had an element of ad hoc bargaining. Chijiwa notes that the "war potential non-possession" clause was connected to a bargain with the Allied powers: Japan gave up its military in exchange for preserving the Emperor system (Â§©ÁöáÂà∂) –ò–Ω—Å—Ç–∏—Ç—É—Ç –∏–º–ø–µ—Ä–∞—Ç–æ—Ä–∞. And this was premised on Okinawa being "fortified" by the US military ‚Äî essentially outsourcing Japan's defense. Article 9 was workable only because America garrisoned Okinawa.

The Real Reason: Escaping the Budget Trap

But Chijiwa reveals that the conventional narrative ‚Äî "Kubo's brilliant theory became the NDPG" ‚Äî is incomplete. Based on recently available oral histories and archival sources, he shows the real driving force was Nishihiro Seiki (Ë•øÂª£Êï¥Ëºù), the pragmatic Defense Policy Division chief.

Nishihiro's problem was simple: the Five-Year Plan system was broken. Each plan required painful budget negotiations with the Finance Ministry, and the Fourth Plan had been slashed so badly it was nicknamed the "Death Plan" (Ê≠ªÊ¨°Èò≤). Nishihiro needed a new format that would eliminate the need for periodic budget showdowns.

The NDPG was created primarily to abolish the Five-Year Plan system, not to implement Kubo's theory. The "Basic Defense Force Concept" was adopted as the justification for switching to the new NDPG format ‚Äî because only a "de-threat" concept could logically avoid specifying a plan period. It was, in Nishihiro's own words later, "a kind of bad habit." The NDPG was, essentially, an "excuse" („Ç®„ÇØ„Çπ„Ç≠„É•„Éº„Ç∫).

The "Unintended Consensus"

Because the Basic Defense Force Concept was so novel and abstract, different factions within the defense establishment interpreted it differently. Hawks read it as compatible with threat-response thinking; doves read it as a restraint on military growth. This ambiguity allowed everyone to claim the concept supported their view. The result: the concept persisted for over 30 years ‚Äî through the New Cold War, the end of the Cold War, and beyond ‚Äî as an "unintended consensus" (ÊÑèÂõ≥„Åõ„Åñ„ÇãÂêàÊÑè) ¬´–ù–µ–ø—Ä–µ–¥–≤–∏–¥–µ–Ω–Ω—ã–π –∫–æ–Ω—Å–µ–Ω—Å—É—Å¬ª.

The Command Authority Problem

The most sensitive issue in alliance management is: who commands whom? –ü—Ä–æ–±–ª–µ–º–∞ –∫–æ–º–∞–Ω–¥–æ–≤–∞–Ω–∏—è In NATO and the US-ROK alliance, the answer is clear: unified command under an American commander. But Japan has always insisted on "parallel command" (ÊåáÊèÆÊ®©‰∏¶Âàó) –ü–∞—Ä–∞–ª–ª–µ–ª—å–Ω–∞—è —Å–∏—Å—Ç–µ–º–∞ –∫–æ–º–∞–Ω–¥–æ–≤–∞–Ω–∏—è ‚Äî each side operates under its own chain of command, coordinating but never subordinating.

The 1952 Command Authority Secret Agreement

In July 1952, at a private dinner at Ambassador Murphy's residence, PM Yoshida Shigeru secretly promised that in wartime, Japan's forces would be placed under a single commander appointed by the United States. This was a verbal secret agreement ‚Äî Japan's defense forces would effectively merge with US forces under the Far East Commander, who also commanded UN forces in Korea and, through them, the ROK military.

This meant the "Japan-US combined commander" would simultaneously be the "US-Japan / US-ROK dual alliance combined commander" ‚Äî linking Japan's forces not just to US forces, but indirectly to the Korean theater. This is the "people-to-people cooperation" dimension of the dual alliance.

This secret agreement created a gray zone for decades until the 1978 Guidelines formally established parallel command, finally ending the ambiguity.

Connection to the US-ROK Alliance

Chijiwa demonstrates that the resolution of command authority in the US-Japan alliance was inseparable from developments in the US-ROK alliance. The "Northeast Asia Command" proposal (1973-75), the creation of the US-ROK Combined Forces Command (1978), and the 1978 Japan Guidelines all occurred nearly simultaneously. The US could not settle command arrangements with Japan without first settling them with Korea ‚Äî and vice versa.

The National Defense Council's Hidden Origin

But the story of how the NDC was created reveals yet another case of institutional compromise becoming permanent constraint.

The NDC was proposed by the opposition Kaishint≈ç (Reform Party), which wanted to create a body through which former Imperial military officers could regain influence over defense policy. Their draft allowed up to one-third of NDC members to be non-civilians ‚Äî a deliberate loophole for men like former General Imamura Hitoshi. This was part of the anti-Yoshida line: forces seeking constitutional revision, rearmament, and autonomous defense.

PM Yoshida Shigeru's faction fought back fiercely. They succeeded in: (1) excluding all civilian members (eliminating the former military officer loophole), and (2) staffing the NDC secretariat with former Home Ministry officials to prevent military influence. The NDC's purpose was officially redefined as "deliberate review for ensuring civilian control." But this was not the original intent of its creators ‚Äî it was the justification the Yoshida faction imposed after defeating the revisionists. This "deliberate review" function then became the defining ‚Äî and constraining ‚Äî characteristic of Japan's national security machinery for the next 60 years.

From "Negative Control" to the Modern NSC

The "deliberate review for civilian control" doctrine led to "negative control" –ù–µ–≥–∞—Ç–∏–≤–Ω—ã–π –∫–æ–Ω—Ç—Ä–æ–ª—å ‚Äî the security apparatus spent more energy restraining defense policy than directing it. The 1972 "Fourth Defense Plan Preemption Scandal" epitomized this: the NDC was used as a watchdog over budget items, not a strategic planning body.

Kubo Takuya (again!) challenged this in 1978, arguing the NDC should be a proactive strategy-making body ‚Äî a real "National Security Council." His proposal was shelved. Only after the 2001 9/11 attacks and the 2011 Fukushima disaster did momentum finally build for a proper NSC.

The 2013 NSC Creation

Japan's NSC was finally established on December 4, 2013, under PM Abe. Its key innovation: a Four-Minister Meeting (PM, Chief Cabinet Secretary, Foreign Minister, Defense Minister) for rapid, focused decision-making, and a National Security Secretariat (NSS) –°–µ–∫—Ä–µ—Ç–∞—Ä–∏–∞—Ç –Ω–∞—Ü–∏–æ–Ω–∞–ª—å–Ω–æ–π –±–µ–∑–æ–ø–∞—Å–Ω–æ—Å—Ç–∏ with ~70 staff from foreign affairs, defense, and police agencies.

Under the NSC, Japan adopted its first-ever National Security Strategy (2013) –°—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è –Ω–∞—Ü–∏–æ–Ω–∞–ª—å–Ω–æ–π –±–µ–∑–æ–ø–∞—Å–Ω–æ—Å—Ç–∏ and the NSS led the drafting of the 2015 Peace and Security Legislation.

The "donut" problem: Despite the NSC's creation, Japan's security machinery remains complex. The NSC handles "national defense" matters; the Crisis Management Director handles emergencies (but not defense); and the exact boundary between "security" and "crisis management" remains blurred ‚Äî a legacy of the deliberate-review-for-civilian-control doctrine.

Key Takeaways for the Future

On the Alliance

The US-Japan alliance must be understood as a component of the "US-Japan / US-ROK Dual Alliance" system. Japan needs to shift from "constraining US forces through Prior Consultation" to "gaining a voice in regional order by playing an active role." With an aging population (Japan as "the old man" in the alliance), the basis of reciprocity must evolve, or the alliance's equality will erode.

On the Constitution

Even after the 2015 legislation, the Minimum Necessary Doctrine still requires drawing lines that may not keep pace with AI weapons, hypersonic missiles, cyber warfare, and other emerging technologies. Whether to amend Article 9 or adopt the Ashida theory remains an open question.

On Defense Planning

The shift from the Basic Defense Force Concept to "dynamic" and "multi-domain integrated" concepts was necessary and positive. But defense planning must continue to evolve ‚Äî and the "exclusively defensive defense" (Â∞ÇÂÆàÈò≤Ë°õ) –ò—Å–∫–ª—é—á–∏—Ç–µ–ª—å–Ω–æ –æ–±–æ—Ä–æ–Ω–∏—Ç–µ–ª—å–Ω–∞—è –æ–±–æ—Ä–æ–Ω–∞ principle may struggle to accommodate emerging military technologies.

On the NSC

The NSC has been a significant step forward ‚Äî but its institutional design still carries the DNA of "deliberate review for civilian control," making it less agile than counterparts like the US NSC.

Introduction ¬∑ –í–≤–µ–¥–µ–Ω–∏–µ
The Core Argument

Japanese strategists face a difficult and highly uncertain international environment. The Chinese military threat has increased dramatically ‚Äî both for Japan itself and for its alliance with the United States. Japan has responded by loosening self-imposed constraints on its military and modestly increasing defense budgets since 2013, but these measures have only marginally slowed a shifting balance of power.

Heginbotham and Samuels parse three ideal-type strategies ‚Äî forward defense, denial, and punishment ‚Äî and evaluate each in light of Japan's evolving circumstances.

Central thesis: Japan's current strategy ‚Äî a forward defense emphasizing traditional force-on-force capabilities ‚Äî was appropriate after the Cold War but is a poor choice today. It leaves Japanese forces highly vulnerable to attack at the outset of conflict and fails to leverage the U.S.-Japan alliance's collective strengths.

The optimal strategy is "Active Denial" (–∞–∫—Ç–∏–≤–Ω–æ–µ –æ—Ç—Ä–∏—Ü–∞–Ω–∏–µ) ‚Äî marrying a resilient force posture and a mobile force in being with limited but potent counterattack capabilities.

This article has immediate relevance far beyond Japan. U.S. military dominance after the Cold War allowed strategists to ignore the distinction between military dominance and deterrence. China's rapid modernization has shattered that assumption. As Cold War strategy demonstrated, however, deterrence does not require decisive military superiority.

Chapter 3
Forward Defense, Active Denial, or Punishment?

Deterrence theories differentiate two fundamental types: punishment (threatening unacceptable losses on an attacker's valued assets) and denial (denying an attacker the benefits of conquest). The authors further subdivide denial into forward defense (defeating attacks in set-piece battles) and active denial (prolonged, active resistance).

Forward Defense
–ü–µ—Ä–µ–¥–æ–≤–∞—è –æ–±–æ—Ä–æ–Ω–∞

  • Defeat adversary at or near the border
  • Concentrated maneuver forces
  • Rapid, decisive force-on-force battles
  • Fleet-based maritime defense
  • Centralized main operating bases
  • Requires superior or equal forces
  • Flexible across conflict levels
  • Fails catastrophically when outmatched

Active Denial
–ê–∫—Ç–∏–≤–Ω–æ–µ –æ—Ç—Ä–∏—Ü–∞–Ω–∏–µ

  • Maintain a "force in being"
  • Continue fighting until exogenous factors tip the balance
  • Defense in depth, yield ground initially
  • Prioritize force survival over maximum destruction
  • Dispersed, mobile, resilient posture
  • Works even when outmatched
  • Relies on allies/time working for you
  • Higher damage tolerance required

Punishment
–ù–∞–∫–∞–∑–∞–Ω–∏–µ

  • Threaten unacceptable pain on attacker's valued assets
  • Strike military or civilian targets in homeland
  • Often associated with nuclear weapons
  • Can use conventional missiles, cyber, SOF
  • Most credible vs. existential threats
  • Hard to justify for limited conflicts
  • Risks escalation
  • Requires escalation dominance

Japan's Strategic Circumstances Today ¬∑ –°—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∞—è –æ–±—Å—Ç–∞–Ω–æ–≤–∫–∞

Relative Strength: Favors China increasingly

Chinese conventional capabilities exceed Japan's in several categories. China can attack bases throughout Japan and as far as Guam (~3,500 km). The PLA cannot invade Japan's main islands (given U.S. alliance), but occupation of southwestern islands is becoming a realistic possibility.

Exogenous Factors: Three work against China over time

‚ë†
U.S. Reinforcements
Forward-deployed U.S. forces are vulnerable early, but reinforcements would swing the balance in a longer fight.
‚ë°
Malacca Dilemma
China imports 60%+ of its oil, 75% through the Strait of Malacca. Only ~20 days of strategic petroleum reserves.
‚ë¢
Domestic Instability
Chinese leaders deeply concerned about domestic unrest. A protracted war introduces new sociopolitical uncertainties.

Three Levels of Conflict

Japan faces challenges at three distinct levels: (1) Gray zone ‚Äî Chinese probes and transit operations, draining Japanese resources. (2) Limited/local conflict ‚Äî combat in the East China Sea (e.g., Senkaku crisis). (3) General conflict ‚Äî Chinese missile and air strikes against Japanese bases to neutralize capabilities.

The authors' conclusion: Given that the correlation of forces in a short war increasingly favors China, that exogenous forces work in Tokyo's favor over time, and that Japan faces scenarios at various conflict levels ‚Äî a mixed strategy with primary emphasis on denial is the best approach.

The operational goal: ensure Japanese forces can absorb attacks and continue fighting effectively, preventing a fait accompli until U.S. reinforcements arrive.

Chapter 5
The Military Problem ¬∑ –í–æ–µ–Ω–Ω–∞—è –ø—Ä–æ–±–ª–µ–º–∞

Japan's current strategic focus is on gray zone activities and localized clashes. But given China's ability to escalate, Japanese strategists need to focus on how to deter escalation during war.

Gray Zone: Costly Overreaction

In 2016, the ASDF flew 851 intercept missions against Chinese aircraft (up from 96 in 2010) ‚Äî an extraordinary burden for 40 F-15s at Naha. Much "gray zone" activity isn't even illegal. The authors argue Tokyo should adopt a more selective, ISR-based approach rather than expensive fighter scrambles.

Limited Conflict: Current SDF Advantage ‚Äî For Now

Japan currently performs well in localized skirmishes ‚Äî 24 years of Aegis experience, 35 years of 4th-gen fighters, and extensive U.S. training. But China is narrowing the qualitative gap rapidly, and in some areas (electronic warfare) has moved ahead.

The Escalation Trap

Critical insight: If Japanese forces were winning a localized conflict, China has incentives to escalate ‚Äî striking Japanese bases and infrastructure with ballistic and cruise missiles to neutralize the SDF's advantage. Just 16 ballistic missiles with penetrating submunitions could close Kadena Air Base for 4‚Äì11 days. 53 cruise missiles could strike all shelters at Andersen AFB (Guam).

Maintaining the ability to survive and fight in a general conflict deters this escalation.

Chapter 6
Resilient Posture ¬∑ –£—Å—Ç–æ–π—á–∏–≤–∞—è –ø–æ–∑–∏—Ü–∏—è
"The most important characteristic of the active denial strategy is a resilient force posture."

Japan should enhance the survivability of its military elements, even at the expense of some offensive capability. The apparent trade-off is illusory ‚Äî concentrated forces optimized for offense are subject to heavy losses against precision strike.

Four Pillars of Resilience ¬∑ –ß–µ—Ç—ã—Ä–µ —Å—Ç–æ–ª–ø–∞ —É—Å—Ç–æ–π—á–∏–≤–æ—Å—Ç–∏

I
Expanded Basing
ASDF operates from ~15 air bases. Japan has 138 airfields, ~80 with runways >6,000 ft. Prepare civilian airports for dispersed operations. Priority: Kyushu & Ryukyu Islands.
II
Mobility & Deception
"Rapid Raptor" concept: 4 fighters + C-17 support at austere airfields. "Untethered operations." Mobile missile batteries on outer islands. Lure missiles at empty shelters.
III
Rapid Recovery
Invest in rapid runway repair and explosive ordnance disposal. Critical for high-intensity missile combat survivability.
IV
Balanced Defenses
Active missile defense is expensive. Mobility, dispersion, and concealment may sometimes be more economical. Luring missiles to empty targets = same effect as shooting them down.

Key innovation ‚Äî Japan's own A2/AD: Japan's archipelagic geography favors creating mutually supporting firing positions. New 300km-range anti-ship missiles from Ryukyu islands create a miniature A2/AD zone (–ø—Ä–æ—Ç–∏–≤–æ–¥–æ—Å—Ç—É–ø–Ω–∞—è –∑–æ–Ω–∞) that can isolate hostile lodgments without costly counterattack.

Chapter 7
Hierarchy of Priorities ¬∑ –ò–µ—Ä–∞—Ä—Ö–∏—è –ø—Ä–∏–æ—Ä–∏—Ç–µ—Ç–æ–≤

Unless Japan doubles or triples its defense budget, forward defense independent of the U.S. is untenable. The priority hierarchy becomes:

1st
DEFEND
Protect key assets enabling government and military to continue functioning. Integrated air/missile defense, ASW, anti-surface warfare.
2nd
ISOLATE
Isolate and strike adversary forces that land on Japanese territory. Use submarines, anti-ship missiles to deny resupply.
3rd
COUNTERATTACK
Retake lost territory ONLY after U.S. reinforcements shift the overall balance. Premature counterattack courts disaster.

Why premature counterattack is disastrous: An opposed landing on the Senkaku Islands would require maintaining air/sea control in the highest-threat zone of Chinese weapons ‚Äî turning mobile assets into fixed targets. Japanese forces would be beyond supporting SAM coverage, within range of hundreds of PLA short-range ballistic missiles, and sustaining just 5-8 fighters on combat air patrol from Naha's 40 F-15s.

For context: in WWII, tiny Wake Island (comparable in size) required 13 Japanese warships, 2 carriers, and 2,500 infantry to capture ‚Äî against 400 defenders and 12 aircraft.

Chapter 8
Long-Range Strike: Limits & Purpose ¬∑ –£–¥–∞—Ä–Ω—ã–π –ø–æ—Ç–µ–Ω—Ü–∏–∞–ª

Long-range strike has gained considerable currency in Tokyo. Three former defense ministers, PM Abe, and two LDP panels have spoken in favor. In Dec 2017, Japan announced studies on acquiring JASSM-ER and LRASM missiles (900+ km range).

The Case For (Limited) Strike

A force of ~300 cruise missiles (~$500M) would give Japan ~5 strikes equivalent to the 2017 U.S. strike on Shayrat Air Base, Syria. With accurate intelligence, strikes on ports might disrupt amphibious operations; strikes on airbases might destroy high-value tankers or bombers.

Three Reasons Strike Should Remain LIMITED

1. China enjoys strategic depth (—Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∞—è –≥–ª—É–±–∏–Ω–∞). It can deploy forces from bases at various distances. Long cruise missile flight times (up to an hour) limit effectiveness against mobile targets. China can simply marshal forces farther from Japan.

2. Countervalue strikes risk escalation (—ç—Å–∫–∞–ª–∞—Ü–∏—è). If Japan targets dual-use facilities (ports, airports), China has escalation dominance with its massive missile inventory ‚Äî even without nuclear weapons.

3. Strike systems invite preemptive attack. China might seek to destroy Japan's delivery systems before they can be used, creating dangerous first-strike incentives.

Authors' recommendation: Acquire a limited inventory of long-range strike weapons as options. But the benefits do not scale up with size. Japan must continue to prepare for an extended, largely defensive campaign within which strike plays a subsidiary role.

But none of these are insurmountable. Japan has proven it can address institutional weaknesses ‚Äî it elevated the Defense Agency to ministry status, created a National Security Council, and reinterpreted its constitution. The key is civilian leadership. As in 1930s Britain, where civilians intervened to prioritize Fighter Command over bombers, political will can override military bureaucratic preference when the threat is pressing enough.

Conclusion ¬∑ –ó–∞–∫–ª—é—á–µ–Ω–∏–µ
The Porcupine, Not the Hedgehog

Japan urgently needs to reconsider its basic approach to deterrence. The optimal strategy is active denial ‚Äî capitalizing on Japan's geography and the defensive potential of modern weapons for the most effective and affordable defense.

Why Active Denial Works ¬∑ –ü–æ—á–µ–º—É —ç—Ç–æ —Ä–∞–±–æ—Ç–∞–µ—Ç

Deterrence: An attack on Japan becomes a protracted fight ‚Äî no quick, decisive Chinese victory is possible.

Stability: Reduces first-strike incentives for both sides. A resilient, primarily defensive posture reduces crisis instability.

Alliance credibility: Signals Japan's status quo objectives. Reassures the U.S., adversaries, and Japan's own public that Tokyo will not initiate aggression. Lessens moral hazard for the alliance.

Affordability: Far cheaper than any plausible alternative. Achievable if Japan raises spending to 2% GDP.

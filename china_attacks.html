Why This Book Exists

Before every Taiwanese presidential election, a wave of military propaganda sweeps through social media ‚Äî rumors designed to inflate PLA capabilities and demoralize Taiwan's public. Since 2019, these disinformation campaigns expanded from niche pro-China forums into mainstream platforms like LINE and Facebook.

The authors ‚Äî military analyst Wang Li and information warfare researcher Shen Po-yang ‚Äî wrote this book to arm ordinary citizens with basic military knowledge–±–∞–∑–æ–≤—ã–µ –≤–æ–µ–Ω–Ω—ã–µ –∑–Ω–∞–Ω–∏—è that can distinguish fact from fiction.

The core problem: Both "blue camp" (pro-China leaning) and "green camp" (pro-independence) voters in Taiwan lack basic military literacy. One side believes China is invincible; the other assumes Taiwan will always win. Both are wrong and both are dangerous.

All propaganda myths share three common manipulation techniques:

1
Ignore Constraints
Present weapons under impossible ideal conditions, then claim those results apply universally. Like claiming anyone can drive Taipei‚ÜíKaohsiung in 1 hour because F1 cars can.
2
Mix Contexts
Combine capabilities that are individually true but mutually exclusive. "China has landing ships" + "China has many boats" ‚â† "boats can carry tanks." Volume ‚â† weight capacity.
3
Linear Projection
Chain individually plausible facts into absurd conclusions, then label any doubt as "ideology." Aircraft carrier exists ‚Üí planes exist ‚Üí Taiwan's east falls. A massive logical leap.

The "Invincible Ballistic Missile" Myth
"Thousands of missiles will reduce Taiwan to ashes within hours" ‚Äî the most persistent and dangerous myth.

The core myth claims China's ~2,000-3,000 short and medium-range ballistic missiles–±–∞–ª–ª–∏—Å—Ç–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∏–µ —Ä–∞–∫–µ—Ç—ã can paralyze all of Taiwan's military infrastructure in the opening hours of war ‚Äî destroying every airbase, radar station, government building, and port.

The Reality: CEP and Explosive Power

Two concepts most people don't understand:

1. CEP (Circular Error Probable) ‚Äî –ö—Ä—É–≥–æ–≤–æ–µ –≤–µ—Ä–æ—è—Ç–Ω–æ–µ –æ—Ç–∫–ª–æ–Ω–µ–Ω–∏–µ

CEP 100m means: if you fire 2 missiles, statistically 1 will land within a 100m radius circle of the target. The other lands somewhere outside. At CEP 50m (generous estimate for PLA missiles), hitting a 20m-wide runway with a single missile has only about 8% probability.

2. Explosive Power vs. Target Hardness

A 500kg warhead that misses a reinforced concrete runway by even a few meters will mostly produce a pressure wave that goes upward, leaving minor surface damage. Missiles must penetrate into the concrete to create real craters. The propaganda substitutes nuclear weapon imagery for conventional explosives.

The Math Doesn't Add Up

Per the U.S. DoD China Military Power Report, the PLA has approximately: 150+ MRBMs, 600+ SRBMs, 300+ ground-launched cruise missiles. But launcher count limits first-salvo capacity to roughly 400-500 missiles at 80% readiness.

Taiwan has 67+ high-priority military targets (11 airbases, 6 major ports, 25 army bases, 20 radar sites, 5 command centers). That's only ~6 missiles per target in the first wave. Using Monte Carlo simulations, you need at least 6 missiles with CEP ‚â§ 50m just for an 84% chance of cutting a 3,700m runway. But fighters only need 600-1,000m to take off with combat loads.

Bottom line: Ballistic missiles are expensive, inaccurate relative to their hype, and not numerous enough to simultaneously neutralize all targets. The PLA cannot fire all missiles at once (launcher limitations), cannot reload quickly, and must prioritize between competing targets. "Thousand-missile first strike destroys everything" is mathematically impossible.

Chapter 2 ‚Äî –ì–ª–∞–≤–∞ 2
The "Thousand Drones Overwhelm Defense" Myth

This myth predates modern drones ‚Äî it originally claimed China could convert thousands of obsolete J-6/J-7 fighters—É—Å—Ç–∞—Ä–µ–≤—à–∏–µ –∏—Å—Ç—Ä–µ–±–∏—Ç–µ–ª–∏ into remote-controlled drones to overwhelm Taiwan's air defenses.

Physical Impossibilities

Converting old fighters to drones faces insurmountable problems: the J-6 uses hydraulic controls (not fly-by-wire), making remote operation extremely difficult. Hundreds of aircraft in the same airspace at similar altitudes traveling at 0.5+ Mach would collide with each other. Radio control has range limits (~150km line-of-sight due to Earth's curvature). Each drone needs a dedicated operator for meaningful combat capability, creating massive manpower needs.

The authors note: if such technology were easy, why wouldn't the U.S. just convert all its aircraft to drones and solve its pilot shortage? The reason nobody does this is that it simply doesn't work well enough for the cost.

Verdict: Manufacturing new cruise missiles is cheaper and more effective than converting old fighters. The "operational airspace" over the Taiwan Strait can only accommodate ~100-200 modern fighters at once. Mass drone swarms of converted fighters remain science fiction.

Very Low

Decapitation Strike Myths: Paratroopers, Helicopters & Civilian Aircraft

Ch.3: Airborne Assault on Taipei

The PLA Airborne Corps has ~49,000 combat personnel across 9 brigades. The Y-20 transport can carry 210 paratroopers, but China only has ~48 Y-20s and 31 Y-8s ‚Äî far short of the 234 sorties needed. Worse: a parachute company of 100 soldiers needs a 2√ó3 km¬≤ drop zone. Taipei has virtually no open spaces large enough. Paratroopers landing on concrete suffer broken bones; on power lines they get stuck; in muddy fields they sink.

Ch.4: Helicopter Blitz on the Capital

The Z-20 helicopter carries ~10 troops. Even 50 helicopters = 500 soldiers. Fifty helicopters cannot fly undetected across the Taiwan Strait in wartime. Helicopters are extremely vulnerable to any anti-aircraft fire ‚Äî even rocket-propelled grenades. Taipei's dense high-rises with power lines make helicopter landing zones nearly nonexistent.

Ch.5: Civilian Aircraft Trojan Horse

The myth: a civilian plane stuffed with 200+ soldiers lands at Songshan Airport and seizes Taipei. Reality: in wartime, no Chinese civilian aircraft would be allowed to approach. Even if one landed, the airport would be under military guard. 200 lightly-armed soldiers would be surrounded and eliminated. Using civilian aircraft as weapons violates international law to the degree that it would give every nation legal justification to shoot down all Chinese civilian aircraft.

All three "decapitation strike" myths require: (1) PLA missiles first destroying all defenses (itself a myth), (2) Taiwan military standing idle, (3) U.S./Japan not monitoring. Remove any assumption and the plan collapses to near-zero probability.

The "Ten Thousand Ships" Myth
"Ten thousand fishing boats carrying a million soldiers will flood across the strait" ‚Äî the most elaborate myth.

China does have thousands of fishing boats. Loaded to capacity, tens of thousands of soldiers could theoretically board. But the myth completely ignores five physical constraints:

1
Weight
A 10-ton fishing boat cannot carry tanks. Only light infantry with rifles.
2
Volume
Soldiers aren't sardines. A small boat fits ~20 armed troops max.
3
Armor
Fishing boats are thin-hulled. Machine gun fire sinks them. Adding armor reduces capacity.
4
Weather
Taiwan Strait avg. 3-5 on Beaufort scale. Hours of heavy seas = troops too seasick to fight.
5
Time
Round-trip takes 8+ hours. First wave must survive alone until reinforcements arrive.

The Beach Problem

Even if troops reach shore, they must wade through water. A fishing boat with 20 troops draws ~1m of water and must stop 6+ meters from shore. Soldiers jump into waist-deep water carrying 30+ kg of equipment, then wade under defensive fire. At ~40% casualty rate per wave (generous to attackers), with 200 boats landing simultaneously on a 1km beach front, the first batch sends 400 soldiers ‚Äî of whom 320 are killed or disabled before reaching defensive positions.

The math is devastating: Even the most optimistic calculation shows first-wave survivors piling up on beaches under withering fire, subsequent waves arriving hours later to find beaches blocked by wrecked boats and bodies. No fishing-boat invasion has succeeded in modern military history. The authors calculate maximum ~300,000 could theoretically be transported in 5 waves, but combat attrition would leave landing forces combat-ineffective long before that.

Ch.7: Aircraft Carrier Pincer Attack –ê–≤–∏–∞–Ω–æ—Å–Ω–∞—è –∞—Ç–∞–∫–∞

China's carriers (Liaoning: 24 J-15s, Shandong: 32 J-15s) must stay outside Taiwan's anti-ship missile range (~300-600km). At maximum J-15 combat radius of 1,270km, subtracting transit time and 16-minute sequential launch time, actual combat loiter time is minimal. The carriers must traverse routes easily monitored by Japan, the U.S., and Taiwan. With limited fuel (returning to Sanya port from Taiwan's east = 2,000km each way out of 7,000km total range), the carrier group is essentially a one-use expendable asset.

Ch.8: Container Ship Missile Platforms

The Soviet Union considered and abandoned this concept decades ago. Using civilian cargo ships as concealed missile platforms is tactically possible but strategically suicidal: it gives every nation legal justification to attack all Chinese civilian shipping. After 9/11, using civilian vessels as weapons is the ultimate international taboo.

Ch.9: Cruise Missiles & Long-Range Rockets

Cruise missiles–∫—Ä—ã–ª–∞—Ç—ã–µ —Ä–∞–∫–µ—Ç—ã rely on gyroscopes, GPS, and terrain matching. China's claims of matching U.S. Tomahawk accuracy are unverified propaganda. In dense urban environments with many obstacles, cruise missiles struggle to navigate to specific targets. Long-range unguided rockets (like Katyushas) are area-effect weapons ‚Äî they cannot precisely hit anything at 100+ km.

Ch.10: Blockading Taiwan –ë–ª–æ–∫–∞–¥–∞ –¢–∞–π–≤–∞–Ω—è

The PLA absolutely cannot blockade open ocean. The volume of shipping through the Taiwan Strait (hundreds of vessels daily from Japan, Korea, etc.) makes interception physically impossible. Port blockade via mining would be an act of war and international terrorism. The Taiwan Strait is too wide, too busy, and too well-monitored.

Every single myth exists because conventional invasion is extremely hard. If the PLA could truly crush Taiwan effortlessly, there would be no need for propaganda. The rumors aim to create defeatism–ø–æ—Ä–∞–∂–µ–Ω—á–µ—Å—Ç–≤–æ ‚Äî making Taiwanese believe resistance is futile so they surrender without fighting. Recognizing this is itself a form of defense.

The Four Phases of a Realistic Invasion Scenario

Ch.11: Victory Conditions

The PLA must achieve total victory within ~6 months to 1 year. Longer timelines invite international intervention. Victory means physically occupying and controlling Taiwan ‚Äî not just bombing it. Three levels: (1) blockade forcing negotiation, (2) limited strikes + political pressure, (3) full amphibious invasion and occupation.

Ch.12: Pre-War Preparations –ü—Ä–µ–¥–≤–æ–µ–Ω–Ω–∞—è –ø–æ–¥–≥–æ—Ç–æ–≤–∫–∞

China must guard against India, North Korea, Vietnam simultaneously ‚Äî only ~half of PLA forces can deploy against Taiwan. Major force movements to coastal areas take 10+ days and are impossible to hide from satellite surveillance. Taiwan's early warning gives time for full mobilization, dispersal of air assets, and naval deployment into the Pacific.

Ch.15: Only ~5 Viable Landing Points

Taiwan's west coast has been increasingly fortified with concrete tetrapods (Ê∂àÊ≥¢Â°ä), shallow mudflats, and urban development. Viable major-port targets are limited to: Keelung/Taipei Port (north), Taichung Port (central), and Kaohsiung/Anping (south). Each is surrounded by hills or urban terrain favorable to defenders.

Ch.16: The Occupation Nightmare

Even if PLA forces land, they face: ammunition incompatibility (different calibers from ROC forces), need for fresh water trucked from ports, urban warfare where every RC building becomes a fortress, and a fully mobilized reserve force. History shows no wargame achieves PLA victory within one year under realistic parameters.

Information War, Civilian Reality & Alternative Strategies

Ch.17: Information Warfare –ò–Ω—Ñ–æ—Ä–º–∞—Ü–∏–æ–Ω–Ω–∞—è –≤–æ–π–Ω–∞

China's political warfare system against Taiwan operates through six major channels: the United Front Work Department, military intelligence, the Ministry of State Security, CCP propaganda department, commercial/military investment channels, and covert online operations. After whistleblower Wang Liqiang defected to Australia, many of these networks were exposed. The goal: manipulate Taiwan's media ecosystem, amplify social divisions, and create defeatism.

Ch.18: War ‚â† Instant Rubble

Most Taiwanese imagine war as instant apocalypse. The authors explain: ballistic missiles target military facilities, not residential areas (civilian casualties create international PR disasters for China). Most citizens would experience air raid sirens, traffic controls for military movements, intermittent power outages, and rationing ‚Äî not Hollywood-style devastation. Communities far from landing zones would experience war more like wartime Britain than Stalingrad.

Ch.19: Alternative Military Options

Seizing offshore islands (Kinmen, Matsu) is possible but strategically counterproductive ‚Äî it would unite Taiwan politically and guarantee international intervention without achieving unification. Such "limited wars" only work if Taiwan's president is already pro-China and uses the attack as pretext for concessions.

Ch.20: Economic Warfare & Blockade –≠–∫–æ–Ω–æ–º–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∞—è –≤–æ–π–Ω–∞

A naval blockade faces the same impossibility problems discussed in Ch.10. Economic warfare (supply chain manipulation, trade pressure) is ongoing but works slowly and can be countered. The authors note that replacing Taiwan in global supply chains would take China's competitors (South Korea, etc.) time but is not impossible ‚Äî meaning economic coercion has limits.

The Strategic Calculus of Major Powers

Ch.21: United States –°—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è –°–®–ê

Taiwan's value to the U.S. is not merely ideological (democracy) but deeply strategic: Taiwan sits at the center of the First Island Chain–ø–µ—Ä–≤–∞—è —Ü–µ–ø—å –æ—Å—Ç—Ä–æ–≤–æ–≤, controls South China Sea access, and houses TSMC. The U.S. maintains an offensive maritime strategy ‚Äî it projects power across oceans. Taiwan falling to China would shatter this entire framework.

The authors caution: Taiwan must not behave like a victim expecting rescue. A nation that "constantly complains that the whole world owes it" will not inspire allied support.

Ch.22: Japan –°—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è –Ø–ø–æ–Ω–∏–∏

Japan's security is directly linked to Taiwan through geography ‚Äî the Miyako Strait and Bashi Channel are Japan's southern maritime lifelines. A Chinese-controlled Taiwan would put PLA forces within direct striking range of Japan's southernmost islands. Japan's increasing defense spending and "Taiwan contingency" planning reflect this existential concern.

Ch.23-25: South China Sea Nations, India & Australia

Southeast Asian nations maintain hedging strategies ‚Äî strengthen defense when China is aggressive, accommodate when China is conciliatory. India views China through the lens of border conflicts and the "String of Pearls" maritime encirclement via Myanmar. Australia, as a maritime nation dependent on U.S.-guaranteed sea lanes, sees Chinese expansion as a direct threat to its security architecture.

China's Real Strategy & Taiwan's Response

Ch.26: China's Fundamentally Defensive Strategy –û–±–æ—Ä–æ–Ω–∏—Ç–µ–ª—å–Ω–∞—è —Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è –ö–∏—Ç–∞—è

The authors make a provocative argument: China's military strategy is fundamentally defensive, not offensive. The CCP's primary fear is regime collapse (like Romania's Ceau»ôescu), not conquest. All ICBMs target U.S. and Russian cities ‚Äî never military bases ‚Äî because the goal is MAD deterrence, not warfighting. Nuclear submarines exist to ensure second-strike capability. The real military purpose is regime survival, not global domination.

Taiwan's greatest threat is not military invasion but internal subversion ‚Äî using bought-off elites and propaganda to achieve "reunification" without firing a shot. "Buying Taiwan is always cheaper than conquering it."

Ch.27: Taiwan's Strategic Response –°—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è –¢–∞–π–≤–∞–Ω—è

Recent U.S. arms sales transform Taiwan's strategic posture: M1 Abrams tanks make light landing armor obsolete; HIMARS rocket artillery and SLAM-ER standoff missiles can strike PLA staging areas across the strait; Stinger MANPADs and mobile SAMs survive missile barrages that destroy fixed installations.

The book's conclusion: Taiwan's defense is viable. No realistic wargame shows PLA victory within one year without fantasy parameters. Taiwan must build genuine defense awareness, invest in asymmetric capabilities, and ‚Äî most critically ‚Äî refuse to be defeated psychologically by propaganda before any shot is fired.

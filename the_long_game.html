Introduction
"Great Changes Unseen in a Century"
"China wants to be China and accepted as such, not as an honorary member of the West." ‚Äî Lee Kuan Yew

In 1872, Qing Dynasty general Li Hongzhang (–õ–∏ –•—É–Ω—á–∂–∞–Ω) wrote that the world was experiencing "great changes unseen in three thousand years" ‚Äî the collision of a weakening China with Western imperial expansion. A century and a half later, Xi Jinping repurposed Li's phrase to declare the world was experiencing "great changes unseen in a century" (ÁôæÂπ¥Êú™Êúâ‰πãÂ§ßÂèòÂ±Ä / —Å—Ç–æ–ª–µ—Ç–Ω–∏–µ –Ω–µ–≤–∏–¥–∞–Ω–Ω—ã–µ –ø–µ—Ä–µ–º–µ–Ω—ã) ‚Äî but this time, the change was China's rise, not its decline.

Rush Doshi's The Long Game argues that since the end of the Cold War, China has pursued a coherent grand strategy (Â§ßÊàòÁï• / –±–æ–ª—å—à–∞—è —Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è) to displace the United States from regional and global leadership. This strategy has evolved through three distinct phases, each triggered by sharp shifts in Beijing's perception of American power.

The Core Argument: China has pursued three sequential "strategies of displacement":

1. Blunting (1989‚Äì2008) ‚Äî Weakening American power over China under "hide capabilities and bide time"

2. Building (2009‚Äì2016) ‚Äî Constructing foundations for regional hegemony under "actively accomplish something"

3. Global Expansion (2017+) ‚Äî Displacing US global leadership under "great changes unseen in a century"

The book is based on an original database of Chinese Communist Party documents ‚Äî memoirs, biographies, daily records of senior officials, and internal speeches ‚Äî painstakingly gathered from libraries and bookstores in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and mainland China. These documents take readers behind the closed doors of the CCP, into high-level foreign policy meetings, and reveal the thinking of Chinese leaders, generals, and diplomats.

Doshi uses the analogy of Eyre Crowe's 1907 memorandum on Germany ‚Äî a famous British diplomatic assessment that tried to determine whether Berlin had a "grand design" running through its seemingly disparate behaviors. Today, the same question is asked about China. Doshi's answer, supported by extensive evidence, is yes.

What Is Grand Strategy?

Doshi defines grand strategy as a state's theory of how to achieve security through intentional, coordinated action across multiple instruments of statecraft ‚Äî military, economic, and political. To identify it, you need three types of evidence: (1) concepts in authoritative texts, (2) capabilities in national security institutions, and (3) conduct consistent with those concepts.

How Does Hegemonic Order Work?

Orders are maintained through three "forms of control" (—Ñ–æ—Ä–º—ã –∫–æ–Ω—Ç—Ä–æ–ª—è):

üó°
Coercion
Military threats, sanctions, deterrence ‚Äî the ability to impose costs on rule-breakers.
ü§ù
Consent
Trade benefits, security guarantees, public goods ‚Äî the ability to provide incentives for compliance.
üèõ

Ideological authority, norms, institutional leadership ‚Äî the ability to command through identity.

A rising power displaces a hegemon by pursuing two strategies: blunting (weakening the hegemon's forms of control) and building (constructing its own). These strategies typically proceed from regional to global scope.

The CCP: A Nationalist, Leninist Party

The CCP is fundamentally a nationalist party (–Ω–∞—Ü–∏–æ–Ω–∞–ª—å–Ω–∞—è –ø–∞—Ä—Ç–∏—è) that adopted Leninist organizational methods (–ª–µ–Ω–∏–Ω–∏–∑–º) as an instrument for achieving its nationalist mission. The Party's goal since its founding has been to achieve China's "rejuvenation" (Â§çÂÖ¥ / –≤–æ–∑—Ä–æ–∂–¥–µ–Ω–∏–µ) ‚Äî restoring China to its rightful place after the "Century of Humiliation" (ÁôæÂπ¥ÂõΩËÄª / —Å—Ç–æ–ª–µ—Ç–∏–µ —É–Ω–∏–∂–µ–Ω–∏–π).

The Leninist structure gives the CCP three critical advantages for grand strategy: coordination across instruments of statecraft through centralized Party control; monitoring through Party cells penetrating all institutions; and enforcement through the disciplinary apparatus. A secret "red phone" network connects roughly 3,000 top Party members directly, enabling rapid top-down coordination.

The Party's hierarchy of guidances structures all policy: the Party line (Ë∑ØÁ∫ø / –ª–∏–Ω–∏—è), the guideline (ÊñπÈíà / –∫—É—Ä—Å), and the policy (ÊîøÁ≠ñ / –ø–æ–ª–∏—Ç–∏–∫–∞). These are set at events like the Party Congress (every 5 years) and Ambassadorial Conferences, and all cadres must follow them.

Chapter 3
"New Cold Wars Have Begun": The Traumatic Trifecta
"It seems that one Cold War has ended but that two others have already begun." ‚Äî Deng Xiaoping, 1989

Before 1989, the United States and China were quasi-allies. The US built and operated two signals intelligence stations in western China to monitor Soviet missile tests. Washington sold China weapons, shared intelligence, and cooperated against the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. But three cataclysmic events in 1989‚Äì1991 ‚Äî the "traumatic trifecta" (—Ç—Ä–∞–≤–º–∞—Ç–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∞—è —Ç—Ä–æ–π–∫–∞) ‚Äî fundamentally transformed Beijing's view of the United States from partner to primary threat.

1
Tiananmen Square
June 1989. Revealed the ideological threat: the US could promote "peaceful evolution" (ÂíåÂπ≥ÊºîÂèò) to undermine CCP rule. Deng called it "a world war without gunsmoke."
2
The Gulf War
1990‚Äì91. Revealed the military threat: US high-tech weapons could annihilate outdated forces (like Iraq's ‚Äî similar to China's). "A vision into a frightening future."
3
Soviet Collapse
Dec 1991. Revealed the geopolitical threat: without a shared Soviet enemy, the US had no reason to tolerate China. Washington became the sole, unchecked superpower.

In response, Jiang Zemin declared at the 1993 Ambassadorial Conference: "The United States will be our main diplomatic adversary [ÂØπÊâã]." This assessment was reiterated by every subsequent leader ‚Äî Hu Jintao in 2006 confirmed the US remained "the main adversary that we need to deal with internationally."

Deng's Response: Tao Guang Yang Hui

Deng Xiaoping formulated the strategic guideline "hide capabilities and bide time" (Èü¨ÂÖâÂÖªÊô¶ / —Å–∫—Ä—ã–≤–∞—Ç—å –≤–æ–∑–º–æ–∂–Ω–æ—Å—Ç–∏, –≤—ã–∂–∏–¥–∞—Ç—å –≤—Ä–µ–º—è) ‚Äî counseling China to avoid confrontation, develop quietly, and never seek leadership. This was explicitly linked to China's weakness: "In the present situation we must absolutely keep a low profile," Deng said, "because to raise the flag would mean to lose." The guideline governed Chinese foreign policy for nearly two decades.

Critical insight: Tao Guang Yang Hui was not a permanent philosophy ‚Äî it was a temporary strategy calibrated to China's low relative power. When that power increased, the strategy was designed to change. As Hu Jintao told the 10th Ambassadorial Conference: "With the development of China's economy and the enhancement of our comprehensive national strength, we will be more flexible and confident."

Chapter 4
"Grasping the Assassin's Mace": Military Blunting
"Whatever the enemy fears most, we develop that." ‚Äî Zhang Wannian, Vice Chairman CMC, 1999

China's military response to the trifecta was to pursue asymmetric "assassin's mace" weapons (ÊùÄÊâãÈîè / –ø–∞–ª–∏—Ü–∞ —É–±–∏–π—Ü—ã, literally "club of the assassin") ‚Äî cheap, asymmetric capabilities designed to neutralize expensive American power projection platforms. The principle was: "what the enemy is afraid of, we develop that."

After the Gulf War, the PLA watched the devastating effectiveness of US precision strikes against Iraq and saw a terrifying parallel with its own situation. The 1993 Military Strategic Guidelines reoriented China's military from preparing for a Soviet ground invasion to preparing for "local wars under high-tech conditions" ‚Äî meaning a conflict with the United States.

Chapter 5
"Demonstrate Benign Intentions": Political Blunting
"An important reason why China now values multilateral diplomacy is US hegemonic behavior after the Cold War." ‚Äî Wang Yizhou, Peking University

At the political level, China joined regional multilateral organizations ‚Äî not to strengthen them, but to stall and blunt them. The goal was to prevent the US from using these institutions to build regional order, organize an "Asian NATO," or promote Western values.

APEC: Join and Neutralize

When the US tried to transform APEC into a vehicle for Asian liberalization and community-building, China's first APEC ambassador Wang Yusheng fought to strip the word "Community" from APEC documents, block security mandates, prevent binding commitments, and ensure the organization remained a toothless discussion forum. China opposed APEC adding any topics beyond narrow economics ‚Äî even HIV/AIDS and drug trafficking. The result: APEC became "irrelevant" during both the Asian Financial Crisis and the 2008 crisis.

ASEAN Regional Forum (ARF): Block and Contain

China joined ARF defensively to ensure the US could not use it to build a security architecture in Asia. Beijing blocked any meaningful institutionalization, preventive diplomacy mechanisms, or conflict resolution capacity, keeping ARF permanently stuck in the "confidence-building" stage.

Launch Alternatives Without the US

While stalling US-inclusive bodies, China helped create organizations that excluded the United States: the Shanghai Cooperation Organization (‰∏äÂêàÁªÑÁªá / –®–û–°) with Russia and Central Asian states, and promoted ASEAN Plus Three (China-Japan-Korea + ASEAN) as an East Asian counterpart to APEC.

The logic of political blunting: China didn't oppose multilateralism ‚Äî it used it instrumentally. By sitting inside US-led organizations, China could veto or slow-walk American initiatives from within. By creating parallel organizations without the US, China could build alternative forums where it had influence.

Chapter 6
"Permanent Normal Trading Relations": Economic Blunting
"The question of MFN status between China and the US is a central issue that will determine the rotation of world history." ‚Äî He Xin, 1993

The trifecta exposed China's dangerous economic dependence on the United States. Post-Tiananmen sanctions and annual Congressional votes on China's Most Favored Nation (MFN) trade status gave Washington enormous discretionary economic leverage over Beijing. China's economic blunting strategy aimed to bind American hands and remove this leverage.

The MFN Fight

Each year, Congress could vote to revoke China's MFN status ‚Äî potentially devastating China's economy. Beijing fought a decade-long campaign to make MFN permanent through "Permanent Normal Trading Relations" (PNTR). This culminated in China's WTO accession, which achieved PNTR and removed the annual Congressional review.

China was willing to make enormous economic concessions to join the WTO ‚Äî concessions that hurt domestic industries and risked political instability ‚Äî because the strategic goal of removing US economic leverage outweighed the economic costs. Premier Zhu Rongji's WTO negotiations were so aggressive that his own colleagues criticized him for giving away too much.

The paradox: The US saw PNTR as a minor concession (China already had MFN in practice). China saw it as an existential priority. This asymmetry in perceived value created the bargaining space that made the deal possible ‚Äî and illustrates how differently the two sides understood the strategic stakes.

Breaking Sanctions

After Tiananmen, China systematically broke the international sanctions coalition by exploiting European fears of "losing market share in China," convincing developing countries to defy the sanctions, and leveraging China's market access as a bargaining chip. These coordinated moves ‚Äî directed from the top ‚Äî succeeded in dismantling the sanctions regime.

Chapter 8
"Make More Offensive Moves": Military Building
"In 2009, China put forward the idea and plan for building aircraft carriers. This indicates China has entered the historical era of building itself into a maritime great power." ‚Äî State Oceanic Administration

After the financial crisis, China's military investments shifted dramatically from asymmetric denial weapons (blunting) to power projection platforms (building). The investments China had deliberately avoided during the blunting phase ‚Äî aircraft carriers, advanced destroyers, amphibious ships, overseas bases ‚Äî suddenly became priorities.

Aircraft Carriers

The story of China's first carrier is extraordinary. In the 1990s, PLA-connected businessman Xu Zengping ‚Äî a former military basketball player turned "red capitalist" ‚Äî was secretly tasked with purchasing the incomplete Soviet carrier Varyag from a Ukrainian shipyard. To conceal Beijing's involvement, Xu posed as a Macau casino investor, bought a luxury villa as cover, and created shell companies. The carrier's engines were publicly claimed to have been removed ‚Äî but were actually "brand new and carefully grease-sealed." After four years of transit delays (including Turkish blockage of the Bosporus), the carrier reached Dalian shipyard. A rumored Politburo meeting in April 2009 officially approved the carrier program ‚Äî and major work began within a month.

The timing is critical: the carrier program was approved in 2009, the very year China revised its grand strategy. China now has three carriers, with more planned.

Chapter 9
"Establish Regional Architecture": Political Building
"It is for the people of Asia to run the affairs of Asia, solve the problems of Asia, and uphold the security of Asia." ‚Äî Xi Jinping, 2014

Where China once stalled organizations, it now began to create them. The most important were the AIIB for economic order-building and CICA for security order-building.

The Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB)

AIIB (‰∫öÊäïË°å / –ê–ë–ò–ò) was proposed at the 2009 Boao Forum by Zheng Xinli, a former deputy director of the CCP's Central Policy Research Office ‚Äî suggesting the concept originated in the Party's highest strategic planning body. Led by Jin Liqun, a former ADB vice president, AIIB launched in 2016 with 57 founding members ‚Äî a diplomatic coup when even the UK joined despite US opposition.

AIIB serves three order-building functions: (1) coercion ‚Äî creating economic dependencies that give China leverage; (2) consent ‚Äî providing infrastructure as a public good; and (3) legitimacy ‚Äî positioning China as a responsible leader. China retains veto power, the bank is headquartered in Beijing, and the non-resident board is a weak check.

CICA: Security Architecture

Xi Jinping used the previously obscure CICA (‰∫ö‰ø°‰ºöËÆÆ / –°–í–ú–î–ê) to announce a "New Asian Security Concept" that directly attacked US alliances: "It is for the people of Asia to run the affairs of Asia" ‚Äî in other words, the US has no role. China and Russia jointly pushed CICA to adopt anti-alliance principles.

The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)

BRI (‰∏ÄÂ∏¶‰∏ÄË∑Ø / –ü–æ—è—Å –∏ –ø—É—Ç—å) is China's most visible instrument of economic order-building. Like Germany's Berlin-Baghdad Railway a century ago, BRI uses infrastructure to reshape the strategic geography of great power competition. The initiative creates three forms of leverage:

Relational Leverage
Debt Dependencies
In Laos, ~20% of the government budget now services Chinese debt. In Sri Lanka, Chinese loan repayments nearly equal total government revenue. Interest rates on Chinese loans (~6%) far exceed Japanese alternatives (~0.5%).
Structural Leverage
Infrastructure Control
Sri Lanka's Hambantota port was leased to China for 99 years after default. China now controls critical infrastructure in dozens of countries. Commercial ports have "potential military uses."
Domestic-Political Leverage
Elite Capture
BRI projects create local beneficiaries ‚Äî politicians, businesses, workers ‚Äî who become advocates for maintaining good relations with Beijing, regardless of broader national interests.

Recognizing the dollar's structural power (the "exorbitant privilege"), China launched campaigns to promote the RMB (‰∫∫Ê∞ëÂ∏Å / —é–∞–Ω—å) as an international currency and to build alternatives to dollar-dominated financial infrastructure ‚Äî including CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System), China's answer to SWIFT. As the head of China's Export-Import Bank noted: "The US used this method [dollar manipulation] to topple Japan's economy, and it wants to use this method to curb China's development."

Chapter 11

"The Western-centered world order dominated by the US has made great contributions to human progress. But those contributions lie in the past." ‚Äî Fu Ying, 2016

In 2016, Brexit and Trump's election convinced Beijing that the West was entering terminal decline. Xi Jinping officially elevated "great changes unseen in a century" in his landmark 2017 19th Party Congress address ‚Äî a 3.5-hour speech in which he declared that China was entering a "new era" and moving "closer to the world's center stage." This represented a clear break from Deng Xiaoping's counsel of restraint.

Chapter 12
"Standing Tall and Seeing Far": Global Expansion
"For our friends, we have fine wine, but for our enemies, we have shotguns." ‚Äî China's Ambassador to Sweden, 2019

China's emerging global strategy has three prongs ‚Äî political, economic, and military ‚Äî each applying the blunting-and-building logic at the global level:

Political: Lead Global Governance

China has aggressively pursued leadership positions in international organizations (heading 4 of 15 UN specialized agencies at one point), embedded BRI and "Community of Common Destiny" language into UN resolutions, and created parallel institutions like FOCAC (Forum on China-Africa Cooperation), SCO, and the "16+1" (now 17+1) framework with Central and Eastern European countries ‚Äî all designed to build legitimacy and coercive capacity.

Economic: Seize the Fourth Industrial Revolution

Beijing sees the emerging AI/quantum/5G/biotech revolution as a historic opportunity. China missed the first three industrial revolutions ‚Äî but aims to lead the fourth. Total R&D spending now roughly equals the US. In quantum computing, China spends at least 10x more than the US. The digital yuan (DCEP / —Ü–∏—Ñ—Ä–æ–≤–æ–π —é–∞–Ω—å) aims to ride financial technology disruption to blunt US dollar dominance.

Military: Global Expeditionary Capability

China is building a global military footprint: overseas bases (Djibouti, with more planned), nuclear-powered icebreakers for polar operations, space and deep-sea capabilities. The 2019 Defense White Paper has an unprecedented section on "Contributing to Building a Community with a Shared Future for Mankind" ‚Äî framing global military presence as providing "public security goods."

Chapter 13
An Asymmetric Strategy for US-China Competition
"The United States has to be as good as or better than its opponent in the effectiveness with which resources are used." ‚Äî Andrew Marshall, Office of Net Assessment

In the final chapter, Doshi argues that the US should adopt an asymmetric competitive strategy ‚Äî one that does not require matching China dollar-for-dollar, ship-for-ship, or loan-for-loan. The goal: deny China regional hegemony while rebuilding the foundations of American order.

Why Alternatives Fail

‚ùå Strategies That Won't Work

  • Accommodation: Conceding regional hegemony invites further expansion
  • Grand bargain on Taiwan: Not credible ‚Äî US can't enforce post-concession
  • Strategic reassurance: China broke promises on SCS, cyber, Hong Kong
  • Engagement/liberalization: Party reverses openness when threatened
  • Regime change: Risks existential war, likely produces worse outcomes

‚úì Asymmetric Competition

  • Blunt Chinese order: Undermine forms of control at lower cost than China's building
  • Build US order: Reinvest in foundations of hegemony ‚Äî alliances, institutions, tech
  • Cooperate where possible: Climate, pandemics, nuclear security
  • Play the long game: US advantages (alliances, demographics, finance, innovation) compound

Help countries scrutinize contracts, train negotiators, block predatory deals. Protect critical technology through export controls, investment screening, and supply chain diversification.
Building
Dollar Dominance + Tech Leadership + Allied Coalitions
Maintain dollar status, lead in basic science research, create democratic coalitions for trade and standards-setting that bypass Chinese influence in global bodies.

Doshi's conclusion: US-China competition is fundamentally about who will lead regional and global order. The US should not try to match China symmetrically but should blunt Chinese order-building at lower cost than China's investments while rebuilding the foundations of American order that Beijing seeks to weaken. History shows that American decline is never permanent ‚Äî "The United States is unlikely to decline so long as its public is periodically convinced that it is about to decline" (Samuel Huntington).

The Sino-American "Double Gamble"
"China and America are playing the largest game of global power in human history."

David Lampton, one of President Obama's most important advisors on China, once asserted that China and the United States are involuntarily engaged in a "double gamble" (double gamble) with history. The United States seeks to maintain its political and economic status; China will rise to challenge the United States as another leading world power in the 21st century.

China is required to bet that America will provide support and cooperation during its rise. America is required to bet that the rise of a strong China will provide a strong, active partner that shares responsibilities in the international arena without threatening American interests.

Liu Mingfu's core thesis: The competition between China and the United States in the 21st century represents a new era in human history. America ‚Äî tough but young ‚Äî and China ‚Äî strong and ancient ‚Äî separated by the vast Pacific Ocean, are playing the largest game of global power in human history.

Liu insists this competition need not be hostile: "While America and China are indeed competitors, they are far from enemies or antagonists. China's dream is not America's nightmare." He envisions a civilized competition ‚Äî like a track-and-field meet ‚Äî where both sides develop and progress through the struggle.

The Track-and-Field Model

Liu proposes a revolutionary framework for the Sino-American competition: the "Track and Field Model" (track and field model) ‚Äî a civilized, rules-based competition where both nations run in parallel lanes without colliding.

‚ùå Previous Competition Models

Eight Differences from the Cold War: Liu argues Sino-American competition differs from US-Soviet rivalry in critical ways: (1) no opposing military alliances, (2) deeply intertwined economies, (3) no ideological crusade to convert the other, (4) no nuclear arms race, (5) different strategic objectives, (6) shared global challenges (climate, terrorism), (7) mutual economic dependence, (8) nuclear weapons make total war unthinkable.

Liu identifies a pattern: America rose to global leadership at remarkably low cost. How America Faces China: A Third Type of Rival

Liu argues China represents a fundamentally new type of rival ‚Äî different from both Japan (ally turned economic competitor) and the Soviet Union (ideological enemy). China is:

Neither enemy nor ally ‚Äî China is the world's most civilized country without extensive military deployments abroad, the largest developing nation with the fastest economic growth, and a nuclear power committed to no-first-use. America has never encountered a rival quite like this, and its traditional containment playbook may not work.

America's Need for Enemies

Liu makes a provocative argument: America needs enemies to maintain domestic cohesion and justify its global military posture. After the Soviet collapse, America cycled through potential enemies ‚Äî terrorism, rogue states, and increasingly, China. Liu cites Thomas Barnett's observation: after 9/11, "China fell off the radar overnight" because terrorism became the new enemy. But as terrorism faded as a unifying threat, China returned to focus.

A Call for the Coming Collapse of China
"The national anthem of New China was written when the Chinese people were at their most vulnerable time. The idea of being prepared for danger in times of peace is the greatest wisdom."

In a surprising final chapter, Liu pivots from optimism to warning. He argues that China's greatest threats are internal, not external ‚Äî and that complacency, corruption, and elite failure could cause China's rise to collapse from within.

The Fate of a Nation Can Change in One Night

Liu cites dramatic examples of sudden national collapse: the French Revolution (July 14, 1789), the fall of the Berlin Wall (November 9, 1989), the dissolution of the Soviet Union. Deng Xiaoping himself warned: "There is no risk in 10 or 20 years, and even more so 40 or 50 years later."

Lessons from the Soviet Collapse

The Soviet Elite Degeneration: Liu traces the decline from Lenin (brilliant theorist) ‚Üí Stalin (ruthless but effective) ‚Üí Khrushchev (reformer but erratic) ‚Üí Brezhnev (who "would sit there blankly, and was not very clear-minded") ‚Üí Chernenko (who could "neither show his own merits nor contribute") ‚Üí Gorbachev (who dismantled the system from within). "The rise and fall of elites determines the rise and fall of nations."

Three American Political Laws That Won't Work in China

Liu concludes by asserting that Western democratization pressure will fail in China because:

1. The "economic development leads to democracy" law: China's economic model does not require Western-style multiparty democracy to function effectively.

2. The "middle class demands democracy" law: China's emerging middle class seeks stability and prosperity, not necessarily Western political forms.

3. The "information openness leads to political liberalization" law: China has demonstrated that internet and information technology can coexist with its political system.

Liu's closing message: China's rise is not inevitable. It requires constant vigilance, competent leadership, anti-corruption reforms, social equity, and strategic wisdom. The greatest danger is not America's containment ‚Äî it is China's own complacency and internal decay. Deng Xiaoping's warning applies forever: "If China, with its one billion people, keeps to socialism and proceeds on the road of peace, it is a force for maintaining peace in the world... But if China pursued hegemony, that would be a disaster for the world."

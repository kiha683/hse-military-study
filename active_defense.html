Introduction
Nine Strategic Guidelines Since 1949
"Since 1949, China has adopted nine national military strategies, also known as the 'strategic guidelines' (ÊàòÁï•ÊñπÈíà —Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–∏–µ —É—Å—Ç–∞–Ω–æ–≤–∫–∏). These guidelines provide authoritative guidance from the Central Military Commission (CMC) for the operational doctrine, force structure, and training of the PLA."

M. Taylor Fravel's Active Defense is the first comprehensive study of all of China's military strategies since 1949. The book asks two fundamental questions: When and how has China pursued major change in its military strategy? And why at these moments and not others?

The answer is a two-part argument. First, shifts in the conduct of warfare –∏–∑–º–µ–Ω–µ–Ω–∏—è –≤ —Ö–∞—Ä–∞–∫—Ç–µ—Ä–µ –≤–µ–¥–µ–Ω–∏—è –≤–æ–π–Ω—ã in the international system ‚Äî as revealed in the last war involving a great power ‚Äî create powerful incentives for strategic change. Second, such change can only occur when the Chinese Communist Party is politically united –ø–æ–ª–∏—Ç–∏—á–µ—Å–∫–æ–µ –µ–¥–∏–Ω—Å—Ç–≤–æ –ö–ü–ö, because only then does the party grant senior military officers the autonomy to formulate new strategies.

Core thesis: China has pursued major changes in military strategy when a significant shift in the conduct of warfare occurs ‚Äî but only when the CCP is united and stable. When the party is divided, even pressing external threats cannot produce strategic change.

Chapter 1
Explaining Major Change in Military Strategy
"What factors prompt a state to change its strategy? By what mechanism is the new strategy adopted?"

This theoretical chapter establishes Fravel's analytical framework. Military strategy –≤–æ–µ–Ω–Ω–∞—è —Å—Ç—Ä–∞—Ç–µ–≥–∏—è is the set of ideas a military organization holds for fighting future wars. It connects means with ends by describing which forces are necessary and how they will be used. Strategy then shapes operational doctrine –æ–ø–µ—Ä–∞—Ç–∏–≤–Ω–∞—è –¥–æ–∫—Ç—Ä–∏–Ω–∞, force structure —Å—Ç—Ä—É–∫—Ç—É—Ä–∞ –≤–æ–æ—Ä—É–∂—ë–Ω–Ω—ã—Ö —Å–∏–ª, and training –±–æ–µ–≤–∞—è –ø–æ–¥–≥–æ—Ç–æ–≤–∫–∞.

The Two-Part Argument

1
MOTIVATION
Shifts in the conduct of warfare ‚Äî as revealed in the most recent great-power war ‚Äî create incentives for strategic change by highlighting capability gaps.
2
MECHANISM
In socialist states with party-armies, the party delegates military affairs to senior officers ‚Äî but only when the party leadership is united.

Why Not Just Threats? –ü–æ—á–µ–º—É –Ω–µ –ø—Ä–æ—Å—Ç–æ —É–≥—Ä–æ–∑—ã?

Fravel argues that immediate external threats alone are insufficient to explain when China changed strategy. China faced the Soviet threat from 1969 onwards, yet did not change strategy until 1980 ‚Äî a full decade later. Threats are often necessary but not sufficient conditions. What matters is whether a shift in the character of warfare has occurred, creating a gap between current capabilities and future requirements.

Party Unity as the Enabling Condition

In socialist states with party-armies –ø–∞—Ä—Ç–∏–π–Ω–∞—è –∞—Ä–º–∏—è (not national armies), the party can grant substantial autonomy to senior officers for military affairs. But this delegation only occurs when the party leadership is united on basic policies and the structure of authority. When the party is fractured ‚Äî as during the Cultural Revolution ‚Äî military strategy becomes distorted by politics.

Chapter 3 ¬∑ First Major Change
The 1956 Strategy: "Defending the Motherland"
"‰øùÂç´Á•ñÂõΩ" (B«éow√®i Z«îgu√≥) ¬∑ ¬´–ó–∞—â–∏—Ç–∞ –†–æ–¥–∏–Ω—ã¬ª

In March 1956, the CMC held an enlarged meeting of senior military officers and issued China's first national military strategy. It outlined how to counter a US invasion –≤—Ç–æ—Ä–∂–µ–Ω–∏–µ –°–®–ê through a strategy of forward defense –ø–µ—Ä–µ–¥–æ–≤–∞—è –æ–±–æ—Ä–æ–Ω–∞ of China's coastal areas. This was a watershed: China's dedication to building a modern military began much earlier than commonly believed.

The Strategic Context

The 1956 strategy was adopted during a relatively stable security environment ‚Äî China did not expect war for at least a decade. Defense spending was declining. Yet China chose this moment to fundamentally transform its military. The principal adversary was the United States, and the main scenario was a US amphibious assault on the Liaodong or Shandong Peninsula in northeast China.

Content of the 1956 Strategy

Before 1956: Civil War Model

  • Light infantry units
  • Mobile warfare on fluid fronts
  • Guerrilla operations prominent
  • No combined arms coordination
  • No air or naval integration

After 1956: Modern Defense

  • Partially mechanized forces
  • Positional + mobile warfare fusion
  • Guerrilla warfare downplayed
  • Combined arms operations
  • Air and naval forces integrated

Operational Concept

Garrison forces would defend fortifications along the coast to slow a US attack. Maneuver units would then be deployed to destroy US forces. The goal: withstand an assault for 3-6 months, deny the US a quick victory, overextend its supply lines, and buy time for full mobilization.

Why 1956? Explaining the Timing

Shift in Warfare
WWII, Korean War & Nuclear Revolution
China absorbed lessons from WWII combined arms warfare and the Korean War (where PLA light infantry faced US firepower). The nuclear revolution demanded fighting "under nuclear conditions." Light infantry was no longer sufficient.
Motivation
Party Unity
Unprecedented CCP Cohesion
The early 1950s saw unprecedented unity within the CCP. No incentives for PLA to become involved in party politics. Peng Dehuai had full autonomy to lead military reforms and strategy formulation.
Mechanism
Not Emulation
China Rejected the Soviet Model
Despite thousands of Soviet advisors and massive aid, China rejected Soviet emphasis on first strikes and preemption. By 1958, CMC decided to adopt only selected Soviet components (weapons tech, combined arms tactics).
Finding

The 1964 Strategy: "Luring the Enemy in Deep"
"ËØ±ÊïåÊ∑±ÂÖ•" (Y√≤u D√≠ Shƒìnr√π) ¬∑ ¬´–ó–∞–º–∞–Ω–∏–≤–∞–Ω–∏–µ –ø—Ä–æ—Ç–∏–≤–Ω–∏–∫–∞ –≤–≥–ª—É–±—å¬ª

In June 1964, Mao Zedong personally intervened to change China's military strategy ‚Äî the only time a top party leader initiated such a change. He rejected the forward defense concept and reverted to the civil-war idea of yielding territory to defeat an invader through protracted mobile and guerrilla warfare.

This was a retrograde change —Ä–µ—Ç—Ä–æ–≥—Ä–∞–¥–Ω–æ–µ –∏–∑–º–µ–Ω–µ–Ω–∏–µ ‚Äî an older strategy replacing a current one. It was driven not by external threats but by Mao's domestic political agenda: his growing concern about "revisionism" within the CCP, foreshadowing the Cultural Revolution.

Why It Happened: The Political Logic

By the early 1960s, Mao had become increasingly concerned about revisionism —Ä–µ–≤–∏–∑–∏–æ–Ω–∏–∑–º within the CCP. He saw party bureaucrats as moving away from revolutionary principles. His strategy was to decentralize economic policy through the "Third Line" construction ‰∏âÁ∫øÂª∫ËÆæ —Å—Ç—Ä–æ–∏—Ç–µ–ª—å—Å—Ç–≤–æ ¬´—Ç—Ä–µ—Ç—å–µ–π –ª–∏–Ω–∏–∏¬ª ‚Äî industrializing China's interior hinterland under the pretext of preparing for war.

But to justify the Third Line economically, Mao needed a military strategy that matched. "Luring the enemy in deep" ‚Äî which assumed territory would be yielded and fighting would occur in the interior ‚Äî was the perfect complement. The Third Line as economic policy and "luring deep" as military strategy were complementary efforts to weaken the party-state bureaucracy.

Strategic Content: 1956 vs. 1964

1956: Forward Defense

  • Hold the coast against invasion
  • Positional warfare primary
  • Centralized command
  • Combined arms modernization
  • Northeast as primary direction

1964: Strategic Retreat

  • Yield territory to invader
  • Mobile + guerrilla warfare primary
  • Decentralized operations
  • Light infantry emphasis
  • No single primary direction

Consequences: A Decade of Strategic Stagnation

The 1964 strategy persisted for over 16 years ‚Äî through the entire Cultural Revolution and beyond. Even after the 1969 Zhenbao Island clash with the Soviet Union made a northern invasion the most pressing threat, the party was too divided to change strategy. PLA officers who wanted reform were stymied by political turmoil.

Key finding: The 1964 case demonstrates how party disunity distorts strategic decision-making. External threats alone could not produce change. The Soviet Union had 50+ divisions on China's border by the late 1970s, yet no strategic response was possible until the party unified under Deng Xiaoping.

The 1993 Strategy: "Local Wars under High-Technology Conditions"

In December 1992, the PLA's high command convened a seminar to examine strategy. By early January 1993, a new strategic guideline had been formulated. Unlike all previous strategies, it was not based on countering an invasion of Chinese territory. Instead, it emphasized how to wage wars over limited aims –≤–æ–π–Ω—ã —Å –æ–≥—Ä–∞–Ω–∏—á–µ–Ω–Ω—ã–º–∏ —Ü–µ–ª—è–º–∏ around China's periphery, characterized by new ways of fighting.

The 1993 strategy is perhaps the most important guideline adopted since 1956. It remains the basis of China's military strategy today (with adjustments in 2004 and 2014). It shifted focus from homeland defense to peripheral contingencies ‚Äî especially Taiwan.

The Gulf War Shock –®–æ–∫ –æ—Ç –≤–æ–π–Ω—ã –≤ –ü–µ—Ä—Å–∏–¥—Å–∫–æ–º –∑–∞–ª–∏–≤–µ

Iraq's rapid defeat in the 1991 Gulf War had a profound impact on Chinese military leaders. The use of precision-guided munitions –≤—ã—Å–æ–∫–æ—Ç–æ—á–Ω–æ–µ –æ—Ä—É–∂–∏–µ, advanced surveillance and reconnaissance, and space-based platforms demonstrated a fundamental shift in how wars would be fought. For the PLA, the message was clear: without high-technology capabilities, any military would be in a "passive position" as soon as war erupted.

But Why Not Until 1993?

The Gulf War occurred in early 1991. The PLA didn't change strategy until January 1993 ‚Äî a two-year gap explained by post-Tiananmen party disunity. After the suppression of demonstrations in 1989, divisions within the party leadership emerged over whether to continue Deng's reforms. The PLA became more politicized than at any time since the Cultural Revolution. Only when unity was restored at the Fourteenth Party Congress (October 1992) could the PLA pursue change.

China's Military Strategies Since 1993: "Informatization"
"‰ø°ÊÅØÂåñ" (X√¨nxƒ´hu√†) ¬∑ ¬´–ò–Ω—Ñ–æ—Ä–º–∞—Ç–∏–∑–∞—Ü–∏—è¬ª ‚Äî The process of moving toward greater collection, systematization, distribution, and utilization of information.

Since 1993, China has adjusted its military strategy twice: in 2004 and 2014. Both represent minor changes ‚Äî adjustments to the 1993 framework, not fundamental transformations. They "enriched and improved" the existing strategy.

The 2004 Guideline –£—Å—Ç–∞–Ω–æ–≤–∫–∞ 2004 –≥–æ–¥–∞

Adopted at a June 2004 CMC meeting. Changed the basis of preparations from "high-technology conditions" to "informatized conditions" ‰ø°ÊÅØÂåñÊù°‰ª∂ —É—Å–ª–æ–≤–∏—è –∏–Ω—Ñ–æ—Ä–º–∞—Ç–∏–∑–∞—Ü–∏–∏. The key wars driving this assessment: the 1999 Kosovo War and the 2003 Iraq War, which demonstrated the integration of information technology into all aspects of warfare.

Informatization ‰ø°ÊÅØÂåñ is China's term for the transition from the Industrial Age to the Information Age. In military terms, it means applying information technology to all aspects of operations: sensors and electronics on weapons, automated C2 systems, cyber warfare, electronic warfare, and psychological operations.

"Informatized" weapons are more precise, more lethal, and ‚Äî when networked ‚Äî enable unified, simultaneous command of disparate forces across vast distances. Information itself becomes a domain of warfare, connecting land, sea, air, space, and cyber.

The main form of operations shifted from joint operations to integrated joint operations ‰∏Ä‰ΩìÂåñËÅîÂêà‰ΩúÊàò –∏–Ω—Ç–µ–≥—Ä–∏—Ä–æ–≤–∞–Ω–Ω—ã–µ —Å–æ–≤–º–µ—Å—Ç–Ω—ã–µ –æ–ø–µ—Ä–∞—Ü–∏–∏. The difference: under the old model, different services coordinated their actions (assigned discrete roles). Under the new model, units from different services are fused together under a single C2 network, enabled by informatization.

As Dean Cheng describes: "The PLA's conception of joint operations has shifted from multiple individual services operating together in a coordinated fashion in the same physical space to unified operations under a single command-and-control network."

The 2014 guideline further emphasized informatization. China's Nuclear Strategy Since 1964
"We also want to build some nuclear weapons, but we are not preparing to make many. When we have the power to counterattack, we won't continue to develop them." ‚Äî Deng Xiaoping, 1978

While China's conventional military strategy has changed nine times since 1949, its nuclear strategy has remained virtually unchanged since 1964: assured retaliation Á°Æ‰øùÊä•Â§ç –≥–∞—Ä–∞–Ω—Ç–∏—Ä–æ–≤–∞–Ω–Ω—ã–π –æ—Ç–≤–µ—Ç–Ω—ã–π —É–¥–∞—Ä with the smallest possible force. This consistency is the exception that proves Fravel's rule.

Why Constant? –ü–æ—á–µ–º—É –Ω–µ–∏–∑–º–µ–Ω–Ω–∞?

The answer is simple: Top party leaders never delegated authority over nuclear strategy to senior military officers. Nuclear strategy was deemed a matter of supreme national policy ‚Äî only the party's top leaders could determine it. Because senior officers were never empowered to initiate change, the mechanism for change did not exist.

Conventional vs. Nuclear Strategy

Conventional Strategy

  • Changed 9 times since 1949
  • Delegated to senior military officers
  • Responsive to shifts in warfare
  • Enabled by party unity
  • Dynamic and evolving

Nuclear Strategy

  • Essentially unchanged since 1964
  • Never delegated ‚Äî top party leaders only
  • Not responsive to external shifts
  • Shaped by leaders' personal views
  • Constant and decoupled from conventional

Nuclear is not integrated with conventional: China's nuclear and conventional strategies have been decoupled —Ä–∞–∑—ä–µ–¥–∏–Ω–µ–Ω—ã. Planning for nuclear weapons has been separated from planning for conventional operations. China's declaratory strategy and operational doctrine envision nuclear weapons use only in response to a nuclear attack, not in conventional conflicts.

Conclusion
Findings, Implications & the Future

Four Contributions

1
First Complete Account
The first comprehensive study of ALL nine strategic guidelines since 1949 ‚Äî correcting decades of Western mischaracterization.
2
Three Major Changes
1956 (forward defense vs. US), 1980 (forward defense vs. USSR), 1993 (local wars with high technology). Each was a new vision of warfare.
3
Warfare + Unity
Major changes occur when shifts in warfare coincide with party unity. Neither alone is sufficient. The 1964 anomaly proves how disunity distorts strategy.
4
Nuclear Exception
Nuclear strategy is constant because authority was never delegated. The exception proves the rule about the importance of the delegation mechanism.

Correcting Common Misconceptions

Misconception
"People's War" Dominated Pre-1980
Wrong. China adopted FOUR different strategic guidelines before 1980. Only two remotely approximated Mao's concept of people's war. The 1956 strategy emphasized modernization and combined arms ‚Äî completely at odds with "people's war."
Corrected
Misconception
"People's War Under Modern Conditions" = 1980 Strategy
Wrong. This phrase was used by some generals but was NEVER part of the official strategy formulation. It only reflected the view that any Soviet war would be protracted. Senior officers first used the phrase in the 1950s.
Corrected
Misconception
1985 Downsizing = Strategic Change
Wrong. The one-million-man reduction was an organizational reform, not a new strategy. The strategic guideline was not changed until 1988, and even then it was a minor adjustment, not a major change.
Corrected

Implications for International Relations Theory

External threats alone don't explain military change. Even when clear threats existed (Soviet invasion capability), change didn't occur without both a shift in the character of warfare AND party unity. China also provides an important case of strategic change initiated by military officers without civilian intervention ‚Äî 8 of 9 guidelines were officer-initiated.
